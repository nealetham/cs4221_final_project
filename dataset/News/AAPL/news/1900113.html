We reviewed the Supermarket Check-in app that shows Sainsbury's, Asda, Aldi, Waitrose, Tesco, Morrisons and Iceland queue lengths

Going shopping during the lockdown can feel like it takes up most of the day

hertfordshiremercury
  • Bookmark
  • Share
What's On
By
Lottie O'NeillMultimedia Journalist
  • 12:56, 27 APR 2020
  • Bookmark

    • We all know shopping at our local supermarkets is tough at the moment.

    From queueing round the block, anxiety on whether you'll be able to nab a trolley, or finding out there is still no toilet paper left once inside.

    Two weeks ago, we found out there was a smart app that tracks queues at popular supermarkets, like Asda, Tesco, Aldi, Lidl, Sainsbury's and Morrisons. It can tell you how long the queue is, what it is like inside and even what the stock levels are like.

    This is a dream come true, right?

    We tested it out on Friday morning, (April 24), to see if it really worked and how close to reality it is.

    What is the check-in app?

    The new Supermarket Check-In app was created to try to help customers across the country, and relies on users inputting data for how long they waited at each store before entering.

    I can be downloaded on Google Play and the App Store, and allows shoppers to check their local supermarkets and stores for the latest queue times, as well as the atmosphere inside.

    However, it works based on customers actively using the app, the more people that download and type in their experience, the better it is.

    What happened when we tried out the Supermarket Check-in app:

    At 9.30am, I downloaded the app from the iTunes App Store and found it was remarkably simple and easy.

    There is no log-in, email or permissions required - which seems to be the case for everything nowadays.

    It asks for your postcode and location and then lists the closest stores that are open.

    Straight away, Wilko's, Morrisons, Asda and Tesco were listed, and even B&Q. Morrisons is my closest supermarket and I have been using it more during lockdown as I've found that I've had better luck with stock levels than Asda or Tesco.

    Seeing a '0' mile queue was enticing, so I headed out a few minutes later, with a list for a family of four as well as items for my grandparents who have been isolating for nearly two months.

    However, when I turned up, the queue was incredibly long and wrapped against itself twice. This is the longest I have seen it.

    Although the line does go quickly as the supermarket has a one-in-one-out policy, it took around 15 minutes before I was near the entrance.

    I've had worse queues, but was disappointed that the app wasn't accurate.

    At least we take queueing seriously in the UK, and see it as a true British past time.

    I tried to check-in on the app, but found that you have to fill it out after you've been inside.

    It asks for queue time inside and at the check out, the atmosphere of the store and stock levels of essential and popular items. This means, depending on how much you need to buy and how busy it is, you could be filling out the information an hour or more later - not really up to date.

    Inside the supermarket

    When inside, it felt chaotic.

    Even though we adhere to the rules of social distancing and respect while waiting to go in, I feel once inside the majority of shoppers lose all of this.

    I was constantly moving out of the way for others because they were coming to close to me or pushing past.

    The in-store experience asks users to click particular options, like if it was 'quiet', 'clean' and 'organised'. You can also select if customer and staff attitude were positive.

    After that you can choose if items were sold out or limited, including fresh fruit and vegetables, meat, bread, eggs, milk and cleaning supplies.

    When I got to my car, it was 75 minutes later, and I filled out the options. Which I feel is not reflective of the time when I arrived at 9.30am.

    Once I had selected my information it did update automatically and the state of the supermarket showed what I described - I believe I was the only one who had actively used the app.

    It does show, underneath the name of the store, the last check-in time in small print, which is very important as you could be reading a check in time for the day before which isn't accurate.

    This app has the potential to help you with your shopping as the pandemic continues, but it relies on users actually checking into it.
