Why you should stop and talk if you see someone wearing a blue coat sitting on a bench in Hull

A new campaign will run this Saturday to get people talking to their neighbours again

    Back in the 'good old days' it was common for neighbours to pop round for a cup of tea and a catch up.

    No one would have batted an eyelid if you popped round asking to borrow some sugar.

    But now, in 2019, many people say they do not even know who their neighbours are.

    For some, this can be a result of social isolation - which has become a major problem in the UK.

    In recent years, there have been increased reports of people feeling lonely and alone, which then prompted the Government to launch the first ever loneliness strategy in 2018.

    During this launch, the Government branded loneliness as "one of the greatest public health challenges of our time".

    Since this, charities and organisations up and down the country have formed plans to change the way we communicate.

    Absolutely Cultured is hoping their new campaign (Chatty Hull) will, once again, get people talking to their neighbours.

    This Saturday, the city centre's benches will be occupied by a blue-coated volunteer who plan on getting people chatting.

    More volunteers will create pop-up tables in tenfoots, streets and parks in Gipsyville and Spring Bank.

    Other events - like a walking tour down Preston Road and 'cakeathon' - will be running all around the city.

    Harriet Johnson, the volunteer from Absolutely Cultured who thought of the campaign, says a small conversation can make a huge difference.

    She said: "In an age when we're spending more time attached to digital devices, having a simple human interaction with someone can do wonders to brighten your mood."

    Harriet hopes people will partake in small acts of kindness, as small as chatting to a random person on the street or a nearby resident.

    "If the person you chat to is feeling lonely or isolated, you might just make their day," she added.
