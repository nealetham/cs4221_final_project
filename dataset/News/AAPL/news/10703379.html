Britons are thirsty to read the news, but still aren’t prepared to pay for it

News may be enjoying a huge spike in readers due to the coronavirus health crisis, but organisations are still struggling to convert readers into paying subscribers

By Ian Burrell
i media columnist
June 21, 2020 6:11 pm(Updated July 13, 2020 2:37 pm)

One thing that is clear from this pandemic is the enormous appetite of the British public for consuming news from trusted sources.

Never before has the news industry had so much reach. National papers are being read – in digital or print – by more than 30 million people every day. That’s up by 18 per cent on a year ago. Add in big local papers and daily readership tops 40 million.

But a second trend is becoming depressingly clear. The British, for all our centuries-long history as a newspaper-consuming society, are becoming world-beating skinflints when it comes to spending money on news (a special thank you to anyone reading this in print).

The Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism last week reported that only 7 per cent of us pay for online news, bottom of a list of 16 comparable countries. Among current non-payers, 50 per cent said “nothing” could induce them to pay in future.

The success of publishers in creating viable business models will determine whether we have a sustainable and diverse news industry in future.

Press created its own problems

The press has undoubtedly contributed to its own problems. The Reuters Institute found that public trust in “most [UK] news most of the time” has fallen by 12 per cent in the past year to 28 per cent. Among left-leaning voters, that trust is down to 15 per cent (from 46 per cent in 2015). The good news for i is that 70 per cent of people said they want newspapers with no political agenda.

Despite falling trust we crave the information news outlets provide. Daily readership of national papers on their digital platforms has grown by 6.6 million in the past year to 25.2 million (up 35 per cent), says the industry body PAMCo. BBC News’s daily web traffic hit a pandemic peak of nearly 30 million in March. But trust in BBC News has also fallen since 2018 – down 9 percentage points to 70 per cent among political centrists and by 20 percentage points to 47 per cent among the very partisan.

The BBC – which still ranks higher in trust among American viewers than their major TV news outlets – is part of the reason people won’t pay for other sources. We still have a vast supply of free online news. The Sun, Daily Mail and Guardian all reach in excess of 35 million UK readers a month. The Independent, Metro and Mirror reach more than 30 million a month. All are primarily open-access models.

More than half won’t consider a news subscription

A study last week by YouGov and the Press Gazette found that 52 per cent of regular news readers in the UK would never consider a subscription. The chief reason given was “free options available” (39 per cent), followed by the desire to buy a printed paper when required (20 per cent).

It’s a different picture elsewhere. In America, the rise of media-baitingDonald Trump triggered a rise in news subscriptions, which has continued as more publishers have introduced online paywalls. Today 20 per cent of Americans pay for news, compared with 14 per cent in Australia and 7 per cent in the UK. In Nordic countries, where news appears to be more highly valued, the figure is 26 per cent, rising to 42 per cent in Norway.

The UK’s embattled local news sector wins little public sympathy. Just 25 per cent of UK respondents told the Reuters Institute they would miss their local news outlet if it closed, compared with 54 per cent of people in Germany and 39 per cent in the US. Local papers take only 5 per cent of UK news subscriptions (most go to The Times and Daily Telegraph).

Search for ad revenue

If readers won’t pay, publishers must find a way to generate the advertising returns that their increased reach deserves. This could happen if advertising brands woke up to the myths of algorithm-generated programmatic campaigns that have been a disaster for the news industry.

A study of 15 major advertisers by PwC and the ad industry body ISBA last month found that these scattergun campaigns feature on an average 40,524 websites (and up to 150,000), many highly obscure. Only 12 per cent of resulting data was measurable and 15 per cent of the marketing budget vanished into an unaccountable black hole.

“There’s a structural problem with the way that programmatic advertising works – a complete lack of transparency,” says Damon Reeve, chief executive of the Ozone Project, which promotes the collective advertising power of the news industry.

Last week James Mitchinson, editor of The Yorkshire Post, suggested a way forward whereby a network of “responsible publishers” and a “responsible advertisers’ consortium” combine to create a healthier ecology where trust and quality prevail.

That’s an idea that all parties should buy into.
