Emergency budget for Birmingham as Covid-19 'catastrophe' raises prospect of service cuts

Birmingham City Council is drawing up emergency budget that could see services cut and taxes rise to plug £212m shortfall

    Birmingham City Council is to draw up an emergency budget in the wake of the 'catastrophe of coronavirus'.

    Services could be cut and council taxes could rise in a 'worst case scenario' to try to plug a £212m shortfall - the equivalent of £186 per person.

    Only a massive injection of cash from the Government, allied to new freedoms to borrow, will ease the impact.

    In a sobering interview, the city's finance chief Cllr Tristan Chatfield warned the 'already struggling' city would have to review all of its spending decisions for the rest of the year ahead and into next year.

    It would do so knowing it was about to face unprecedented demand for housing, homeless services, benefits and bailouts amid scathing job losses and a widely predicted recession.

    He would not be drawn on what services were most at risk but, to put it into context, a £212m shortfall represents a quarter of the council's total net budget this year of around £850m.

    This could mean an impact on any or all of the council's services - from bin collections to schools, pothole repairs to libraries, youth centres to day centres for the elderly.

    The city's capital investment programme, including projects designed to boost jobs and economic regeneration, will also be reviewed.

    Cllr Chatfield also warned the Government was being pressed to consider lifting the cap on council tax rises which, though unpalatable, would be another way for the council to raise vital income.

    The city's finance team, led by chief finance officer Rebecca Hellard, is preparing a report for the council's Cabinet, which is next due to meet on July 21.

    Cllr Hatfield said options were "limited".

    "We can put up council tax or borrow more, which is expensive in the long term, or introduce more cuts.

    "We were intending to introduce a six monthly budget review process anyway, as good practice, but clearly the Covid crisis has hastened our hand.

    "We will now hold an emergency budget to find a way through this. "

    And he warned: "The scale of it will be in the governnment's hands."

    But Cllr Chatfield ruled out a Section 114 notice - a legal device being considered by some councils because they have run out of money, essentially signalling bankruptcy.

    "We don't think it would help," he said.

    "A Section 114 is there as a means to stop councils continually over-spending, by imposing emergency controls on spending. It is generally based on an assumption that the council has done something wrong, and failed to control spending.

    "This is not like that. This is a unique set of circumstances.

    "But we need to know how far Government is going to go to prevent local authorities slipping into bankruptcy."

    Cllr Chatfield said the city council, along with other local authorities, was imploring the Government to hold true to its promise to ensure councils got 'whatever it takes' to pull through the coronavirus pandemic.

    We reported last month how Birmingham's council leader Cllr Ian Ward had reacted angrily to news that the government would not, after all, commit to footing the whole bill for its Covid-19 spending.

    Cllr Chatfield said: "Communities minister Robert Jenrick made a clear pledge - it was not mealy mouthed or uncertain, it was clear - we will do whatever it takes to support councils.

    "We took that pledge at face value, as did other councils, and since then the government has very carefully backtracked.

    "They have said 'we have got your back - up to a point'. "

    The city has so far received £70.3m in two lots of emergency funding grants to help meet its Covid-19 spending.

    Said Cllr Chatfield: "It's looking extremely unlikely we can sustain a balanced budget without the extra money spent and income lost."

    At the start of this year, the city council was in good financial health, and all eyes were on the Commonwealth Games in 2022 and High Speed 2 as economic drivers for the city and region.

    But now the city is braced for the worst, unless there is a huge cash injection.

    "The economic impact of job losses is going to be huge - for example, Swissport handle baggage at Birmingham Airport and have just made huge job losses.

    "They are going to rocket and many families will be struggling as a result.

    "That will mean more need for benefits, housing pressures, drug and alcohol services, mental health - the knock on impact is huge.

    "There is clearly a need for a substantial review of our budget plans. Normally we could move money around and react to emergencies but this situation is way beyond that.

    "We have essentially closed the city down for three months, and the impact of that will be felt for many years, before we even factor in the risk of local lockdowns.

    "We cannot wing this, there has to be a proper plan for the years ahead. Then we need to be open and honest with our residents about what we are doing, and why, and that is what we will do."

    England's Minister for Local Government Simon Clarke MP said: “We’re giving councils an unprecedented package of support, including £3.2 billion non-ringfenced emergency funding, to tackle the pressures they have told us they’re facing.

    “This is part of a wider package of support from across government for local communities and businesses - totalling over £27 billion - including grants, business rate relief and for local transport.


    “We are working on a comprehensive plan to ensure councils’ financial sustainability over the financial year ahead - we will continue to work closely with them to ensure they are managing their costs and we have a collective understanding of the costs they are facing.”
