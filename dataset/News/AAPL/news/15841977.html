Government halts every plan to open new smart motorway over safety fears

The M4 through Berkshire is in the middle of being upgraded

    The Government has halted the opening of any new smart motorways over safety fears .

    Transport Secretary Grant Shapps has announced that no new smart motorways, including a £92 million project in Kent, will not open until a Government safety review is complete.

    It comes days after a BBC Panorama investigation found 38 people were killed on a smart motorway in the past five years .

    Smart motorways are designed to increase capacity and reduce congestion in particularly busy areas by converting the hard shoulder into a running lane, and using variable speed limits to control traffic.

    Work is currently underway to convert the M4 into a smart motorway throughout its stretch through the county.  The M4 runs by many of the area’s key towns, including Bracknell , Maidenhead , Slough and Reading , Berkshire.

    Highways England has been contacted for more information on the impact Mr Shapps’ announcement will have on the M4 upgrade.

    Highways England releases statement

    Highways England has confirmed work will continue on the M4 as part of the upgrade to a smart motorway.

    A spokesman said the authority had sought clarification from the Deaprtment for Transport, and that all projects currently underway will continue - but any completed works will not be opened until the review had taken place.

    The spokesman said: “Any projects currently underway, including the M4, will continue. But any completed roads will not open until after the review.”

    What is a smart motorway?

    Smart motorways involve using the hard shoulder for live traffic and have been developed by Highways England as a way of increasing capacity without the more costly process of widening roads.

    Parts of the M1, M4, M5, M6, M42 and M62 have been converted.

    But there are growing concerns that the removal of a permanent hard shoulder has created a safety hazard, with some drivers being killed after stopping in live running lanes.

    Recap of the decision

    No new smart motorways will be opened until a Government safety review is completed, the Transport Secretary has announced.

    Grant Shapps told the Commons that a £92 million project in Kent is among those that will not open to traffic while the dangers of the roads are assessed.

    Work to convert a 6.5-mile stretch of the M20 into a smart motorway between West Malling and Aylesford was due to be completed in March after two years of work which has disrupted journeys.

    Chatham and Aylesford MP Tracey Crouch asked Mr Shapps if he agreed that the new road system should not open because it does not have stopped vehicle detection systems or “appropriately spaced emergency refuges”.

    The Transport Secretary replied: “That stretch of the M20 and all other stretches which are currently being worked on will not be opened until we have the outcome of the stock take.”

    What Highways England has said

    Highways England has been contacted for more information about how the halt affects plans to upgrade the M4.

    A spokesman for the authority has previously lauded smart motorways. It claims smart motorways have improved journey reliability by 22 per cent and personal injuries have been reduced by half.

    They want to further educate drivers on how to use smart motorways, stressing the importance of understanding and following the instructions.

    There are around 200 miles of smart motorways in operation across the UK, which has about 2,173 miles of motorway. Plans are in place to make a further 300 miles of motorway “smart” by 2025.

    Smart motorways in the state they are now serve as cost cutting exercises. In cases where the roads need widening it’s cheaper to adapt the hard shoulder into another lane and include periodic lay-bys.

    If smart motorways aren’t following the design that initially led ministers to approve the rollout and are making people less safe then what good are they really good for? If they’re not safe then they’re not fit for purpose.

    What are the concerns?

    There are major concerns from the police and the AA over the schemes because without hard shoulders, drivers have to stay in their cars and hope they are spotted.

    If they are spotted, the lane is closed so the police and Highways Agency can remove the vehicles safely.

    Cameras monitor the lanes and overhead gantries display a red X if a lane is closed.

    Instead of the hard shoulder, “refuges” are provided at points along the road.
