Why Trump’s Potential WeChat Ban Will Tank Future iPhone Sales In China

Citing the need to safeguard national security, U.S. President Donald Trump released a pair of executive orders that, once they kick into effect in 45 days, will prohibit American companies from working with Chinese apps WeChat and TikTok.

Announced as part of an order named “Clean Network,” the orders prohibit U.S. residents from doing business with the two Chinese apps, meaning that they will not be available to be downloaded on Apple’s App Store or Google’s Play store.

While TikTok is highly popular, it is the WeChat ban that could have unintended consequences for Apple. Owned by Shenzhen-based Tencent, WeChat is ubiquitous in China, with a user base of over 889 million inside the country according to four-year-old stats (the number is likely over a billion today) and over 1.2 billion total users worldwide.

Anyone who’s spent time in China will likely agree that it is virtually impossible to live in China and not use WeChat: it is more than just a messaging app, but also a digital wallet, a news portal, an online sales hub and more.

If WeChat is removed from Apple’s App Store, this makes the device a very hard sell to Chinese consumers. Android users will be fine, because Android allows users to install apps outside of the official Google Play Store (a method known as “side-loading”). But iOS does not allow side-loading unless the device has been “jailbroken,” a.k.a. hacked.

China is hugely important to Apple’s bottom line. According to research by Canalys last year, 20% of Apple’s total revenue comes from China, and 16% of all iPhones made are for the Chinese market. In Q1 of this year, Apple generated $13.58 billion from just iPhone sales in China alone, according to Apple’s Q1 2020 earnings results.

It would be fair to assume iPhone sales in China will decline sharply if, say, the next model of iPhone 12 cannot install WeChat. The good news, for now, is that no one knows how Trump’s Clean Network program can be implemented. No technical detail or specific rules have been announced, and an expert on international tech policy told tech site The Vergein an interview that the “specifics [of the program] don’t add up terribly well; they don’t speak to a good understanding of how networks function, or a very clear idea of how this is expected to be implemented.”

Whatever the case, we can be sure that Apple is watching this new potential ban closely. And if iPhone will not be able to install WeChat in the near future, the major winner of Trump’s move would be, ironically, Huawei, whose phones account for 36% of China’s mobile market share.
