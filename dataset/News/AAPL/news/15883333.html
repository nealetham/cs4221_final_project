Coronavirus second wave: How a new spike in UK Covid-19 cases can be avoided, according to experts

More than 10 million people have tested positive for Covid-19, with more than half a million deaths

By Ruchira Sharma
June 29, 2020 5:31 pm(Updated July 13, 2020 2:27 pm)

The term “second wave” is being used inappropriately for countries that are experiencing a delayed first wave, according to experts.

Dr Keith Neal, emeritus professor in the epidemiology of infectious diseases at the University of Nottingham, says global increases in cases numbers are driven by rising figures in India and America – but that these increases are really the first wave as coronavirus has taken longer to get some countries than others.

“Globally we’re still at the first phase of the pandemic,” Dr Neal said.

It comes as the world hits a sombre milestone: more than 10 million people have tested positive for Covid-19. Latest figures suggest more than a half a million have died from the disease.

A quarter of the world’s cases are in the US, and the numbers continue to rise.

First stage

New outbreaks may look like a second wave, but the first wave hasn’t really ended yet, said Andrew Conlan, senior lecturer in Epidemiology at Cambridge University.

Before the pandemic the term “second wave” was used to describe a second outbreak after the first one ended due to herd immunity effects, either because of seasonal changes or the number of people infected. In this pandemic, countries across the world have introduced extreme social distancing measures to drive the rate down, which is different from herd immunity.

Experts are unsure how likely a second wave is in the UK. The likelihood depend on the kinds of precautions, such as lockdown measures, masks and other preventative measures, put in place.

It’s very likely in the western world “there won’t be a second wave because we know how to stop it with lockdowns,” explained Dr Neal. “There may be an increase in cases once we lift restrictions and realise we may have gone too far but then we can reimpose them.”

However Dr Conlan said that a second wave is “likely” because the virus was only stopped when the UK imposed a severe lockdown.

“A second wave of some sort is likely given that the first wave only ended due to the extraordinary extent of social distancing that was imposed by the lockdown,” he explained. “The size and extent of the second wave will depend on both our own behaviour with respect to the extent we continue to social distance, but also with respect the effectiveness of the new test and tracing policies.”

Different to first wave

One of the big questions is what a second wave would look like.

Dr Conlan said it would be different to the first. “The big difference now is that communities that experienced high levels of transmission in the first wave should now have some level of herd immunity”.

He said that the higher infection rate in a community, the lower the rate of spread that can be expected during a second wave.

“Overall we would expect this to slow the speed of spread of a second wave, but also increase the variability between regions,” he said. However, there are still many unknowns about the virus, Dr Conlan said, including key factors such as the duration and strength of immunity.
