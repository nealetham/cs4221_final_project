Why feminism's place is (finally) on the screen

ByStephanie Bunbury
June 19, 2020 — 10.00am
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.

More than a century after women won the vote, a group of British women finally made a film about the movement that made it happen. Sarah Gavron's Suffragette, written by the much-lauded Abi Morgan, featured Meryl Streep as the voice of the reformers, Emmeline Pankhurst. Gavron's focus is not on the leaders, however, but on the unsung factory girls and middle-class wives who marched in the streets, went to jail and died in hunger strikes.

"I feel like this is the film I hadn't seen, telling these women's story, understanding what they went through," she said when the film came out in 2016. "I just feel it has to be out there."

What took so long? It's not a rhetorical question. The fight for women's suffrage was clustered with dazzling personalities, derring-do, political intrigue and moving personal histories: enough material for dozens of films. Think how many films have been made – and continue to be made – about a contemporary event, the First World War. Lest we forget, the women's fight wasn't just about voting, but divorce, factory conditions, contraception and a woman's right to her own property. About everything, you might say, but not enough to get a movie made about it.

"There is no such thing as women's history," said Meryl Streep, promoting the film with Gavron. "There is history, which women have been shut out of."

Four years later, however, we're in the middle of the #MeToo era, another slice of history authored by women. Last year's Bombshell, which starred Charlize Theron and Nicole Kidman as Megyn Kelly and Gretchen Carlson, the Fox News anchors who were the most famous of 23 women whose accusations of sexual harassment led to the sacking of network founder Roger Ailes, was described as "the first #MeToo movie". Ailes was also the subject of an acclaimed mini-series, The Loudest Voice, featuring Russell Crowe in a performance so uncannily and unpleasantly exact that you felt you needed to shower after each episode.

Five films about Harvey Weinstein's life of crime are currently in the works, two of which – one by Scarface director Brian De Palma, one under the auspices of Brad Pitt's company Plan B – will be dramas, while the rest will be documentaries. They are gloriously preceded by Australian director Kitty Green's The Assistant, an austere account of a day in the life of a personal assistant called Jane (Julia Garner) in a New York film production office where the reigning culture is one of bullying.

The boss is never named and never seen – although he shouts a good deal – but we know what this is about. Behind his office door, he is doing things to young women hoping for a break. Everyone knows but it is as if they are immobilised by the forcefield of fear the boss has created around him. Slow and modest it may be but The Assistant has been a breakthrough hit at every pre-COVID festival where it has been shown.

If anyone still doubted that this was the year of #MeToo, that doubt would have disappeared when the new streamer Apple TV+ launched with The Morning Show as its flagship offer. Renamed Morning Warsin Australia, the show is a compellingly soapy, overwrought account of a breakfast-show host sprung for sleaze, as Matt Lauer was at NBC.

Jennifer Aniston is the co-host who has lost her on-air partner of 14 years, Reece Witherspoon his replacement ring-in. The predator is played by Steve Carell, a brilliant casting choice given that he is nothing but likeable: #Notallmen is nonsensical special pleading, obviously, but surely #NotSteveCarell?

These films and television shows are surfing the moment. Campaigns and court cases around the entertainment industry have caught the public imagination for obvious reasons: they involve famous names, familiar faces and the perennial fascination we feel with the secret unhappiness of people who seemingly have it all. Of course, every industry has its Harvey Weinstein, every office its Matt Lauer, but they don't have the same tarnished glamour. These stories come to us camera-ready; no wonder the industry has been quick to take them back.

But there's something else. Along with the #MeToo spin-offs, there is also a new and burgeoning interest in the history of the much more defiantly women's movements that made #MeToo possible. A Google search for "feminist film" will bring forth female superheroes, numerous lists of films to watch with your mum on International Women's Day and, inevitably, a still from Ridley Scott's Thelma and Louise.

Films actually about feminism, however, are another matter. Until this year, Suffragette was a lonely outlier. The women's liberation movement of the '70s – the movement that read Betty Friedan's The Feminine Mystique and Germaine Greer's The Female Eunuch, that burned bras and ditched men for lesbian separatist collectives on the one hand and staged heroic strikes for equal pay on the other - has been passed over altogether. A new generation could hardly be expected to know it existed.

It was clear that tide had turned at the Sundance Film Festival in January, where The Assistant had its debut alongside The Glorias, Julie Taymor's biopic of 86-year-old American activist and founder of Ms. magazine Gloria Steinem. Four actors play her: as a child on the wrong side of the tracks, in her ambitious teens, her roaring 20s (Alicia Vikander) and her ferocious 40s (Julianne Moore). This is Steinem's story; reviews suggest that it is effectively more of a biography of the movement itself. Steinem was there to pump the film with Taymor; charismatic and clever, still the star of any show she attends, she was a Democratic Party insider at a time when the women's movement achieved more clout in government than at any time before or since.

Watching the dynamic Steinem charm her interviewers on the Sundance stage, I remember Suffragette director Sarah Gavron saying that she had considered making a biopic about Emmeline Pankhurst and decided against it.

"I think that there is an amazing story to be told," she said. "But we thought that would be the story of an exceptional woman and inevitably, partly a story about power." That is certainly Steinem's story. But a few months later, another film popped up in Britain that looked at '70s women's lib from the reverse angle: Philippa Lowthorpe's Misbehaviour.

Practically nobody has heard of the young women who stormed the theatre where the 1970 Miss World competition was being televised but a hundred million people – a bigger audience than the one that watched the moon landing – saw them scare Bob Hope off the stage and unfurl banners protesting against the objectification and humiliation of women judged for the bounce in their behinds. Keira Knightley plays Sally Alexander, a mature-aged student who throws in her lot with a radical women's collective. It's not always a good fit; Sally has a husband, child and a mother whose despised life as a housewife has left her handily available to babysit. "Even in jail," says one of her new comrades, "you're the most middle-class person I know."

Sally Alexander went on, we are told in the end titles, to become a university professor. The winner of Miss World, Jennifer Hosten of Grenada (played with authority and elegance by Gugu Mbatha-Raw, who also has a pivotal role in Morning Wars) became the first woman of colour to win the crown, then became her country's ambassador to Canada. There is no question what the film thinks of beauty competitions but a provocative parallel is drawn between the two groups of young women striking out into the world on either side of the same battle.

On television, a fictionalised Gloria Steinem turns up again in the excellent Mrs America, an account of the campaign throughout the '70s for an Equal Rights Amendment to the American Constitution. In 1970, even President Nixon was on board; the battle was to persuade sufficient states to ratify it. Steinem (played here by Rose Byrne), Betty Freidan (Tracey Ullmann), African-American presidential candidate Shirley Chisholm (Uzo Aduba) and the ebullient Bella Abzug (Margo Martindale) led the campaign. Ranged against them are recalcitrant males of all political persuasions – both the Republican old guard and Democratic nominee George McGovern's "Georgia boys" – and an army of militant housewives led by Phyllis Schlafly.

Cate Blanchett plays Schlafly as a heightened monstrosity, witchily animated by thwarted ambition; her real area of enthusiasm is military policy but her right-wing brethren are never going to give her a seat at that table. Opposing the amendment gives her a chance to shine. The series has been glowingly reviewed but Gloria Steinem has dismissed it as "ridiculous, silly and not very good".

The amendment, Steinem says, was defeated by the insurance industry; there is no evidence Schlafly changed one vote. "The series makes it seem as if women are our own worst enemies, which keeps us from recognising who our worst enemies are. Not that we aren't in conflict – yes, we are in conflict – but, by and large, we don't have the power to be our own worst enemies."

Of course, history is never fixed. Which stories are told and the way they are told can – and arguably should – be constantly disputed. "I'm not a big fan of Bombshell," says Kitty Green. "It's sensational in a way I find unsettling. The way they chose to cover it, I felt they ignored the broader systemic issues; it focused on some high-powered people with problems as opposed to looking at larger systems that keep women out of power and keep men in. We got rid of Harvey Weinstein but the culture that keeps these predators in power and allows them to do what they do still exists."

Misbehaviour feels close to the truth of my youth but it's cosy and funny and very English – "like Sunday afternoon television" said a fellow film writer. It was about a revolution; he wanted it to feel revolutionary.

But maybe it does do something revolutionary, just by remembering. Film has paid so little attention even to its own feminist history. We all know how pathetically few Hollywood films are directed by women – 4 to 6 per cent each year. But in the silent era, when the industry was maverick and new and nobody had set any rules, half the scripts were written by women. In 1916, the highest-earning director was a woman, Lois Weber, who had a running rivalry with D.W. Griffith.

But while Griffith's epic Birth of a Nation remains a fundamental – if fiercely contested – text for film students all over the world, Weber's name is forgotten by all but a few film historians. Is that obstructive ceiling really made of glass, or is it more like a cloud of forgetfulness? If women are going to change anything now, we need to remember how things once were, how they changed and who changed them. And we need to see movies about that.

The Assistant is screening at selected cinemas from June 22; Q&A sessions with Kitty Green will be held at Classic Elsternwick on June 22; Lido Cinemas on June 23; and Cinema Nova on July 7. The Loudest Voice is on Stan. Mrs America is on Foxtel. Misbehaviour will be in cinemas later this year.
