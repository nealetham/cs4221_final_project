NVIDIA and Apple Lead S&P 500 Gains Amid Regulatory Challenges for Funds

GuruFocus News
Oct 25, 2024

This year, the U.S. stock market has seen an unprecedented concentration, with technology giants like NVIDIA (NVDA) and Apple (AAPL, Financial) contributing nearly half of the S&P 500's growth. Major investment funds such as Fidelity and T. Rowe Price have been compelled to sell off technology stocks to comply with regulatory requirements that mandate no more than 50% of a portfolio be concentrated in large-cap stocks. These regulations aim to encourage diversified investment strategies.

According to current reports, the IRS mandates that regulated investment companies must ensure that a single stock does not compose more than 5% of a portfolio, and the collective large-cap holdings must not exceed the 50% threshold. This rule poses a significant challenge, given that NVIDIA, Apple, Meta (META), Microsoft (MSFT), and Amazon (AMZN) alone have contributed approximately 46% to the S&P 500's rise this year.

The concentration in large-cap tech stocks means that funds wanting to increase stakes in companies like NVIDIA and Apple risk breaching the regulatory red line. A recent report highlighted that by the end of September, Fidelity's $67 billion Blue Chip Growth Fund had over 52% of its portfolio in top-heavy stocks, including NVIDIA, Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, Alphabet (GOOGL), and Meta.

Similarly, BlackRock's recent U.S. Long Duration Fund showed a 52% allocation in assets exceeding the 5% threshold. T. Rowe Price's $63 billion Blue Chip Growth Fund had surpassed the 50% limit in six of the past nine months. However, they rebalance at the end of each quarter to meet IRS regulations.

The increasing concentration towards the 50% limit makes it difficult for funds to amplify holdings in large-cap stocks. For instance, the Ark Innovation ETF holds 43% of its assets in large-cap stocks. According to Hamilton, a partner at Faegre Drinker law firm, reorganizing holdings may negatively impact fund performance, as selling large amounts of stock is not ideal.

Jim Tierney, a growth portfolio manager at Alliance Bernstein, explains that it is a difficult situation for active funds. Normally, holding 6% to 7% in a high-confidence company is acceptable, but these proportions are now considered standard or even low, marking an unprecedented scenario.
