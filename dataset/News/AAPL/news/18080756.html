The battleground Hull seat which is one to watch in the general election

Labour holds the smallest majority here of all the Hull seats - and there is growing disaffection over Brexit

    In more ordinary times, seats like Hull West and Hessle would not be getting much attention in this election.

    For decades, the seat formerly held by Labour stalwart Alan Johnson has voted one way and one way only, and has been put firmly in the category of Labour stronghold.

    However, there is of course a new word that has been factored into that description: Leave.

    Hull West and Hessle, currently held by Labour's Emma Hardy, voted to leave the EU by 67.8 per cent.

    What's more, the constituency has the smallest majority of all the Hull seats, at just 8,000. In Hull East it is about 10,000 and in Hull North it is around 14,000.

    For these reasons, Hull West and Hessle is a target for both the Tories and the Brexit Party.

    Both think that in light of the unique circumstances of this election, they have a good chance of pulling votes away from Labour, the party that has ruled here for decades.

    Switching sides

    The Tories believe that for the first time ever, there is a chance they could win here.

    Their candidate for Hull West, Scott Bell, admits that when he was first selected he was concerned that a historic aversion to the Tories here would make it difficult to get his message out.

    Added to that is the fact that there has been nine years of austerity under the Conservative government, something he has been challenged about by voters on the doorstep.

    But Mr Bell told Hull Live that he thought people were reconsidering their loyalties.

    He said he had managed to switch a few voters over to his party.

    He has been targeting Labour voters in the inner city, such as in the Boothferry and St Andrew's and Docklands wards, once traditional fishing zones, and he will hold a street surgery in the area on Saturday.

    General election 2019

    The B word

    "In a constituency that voted 68 per cent to leave, Brexit is the main issue," he said.

    "The Boris brand goes down stronger here than maybe the Conservative brand," he said. "For the first time ever, people are taking time to reassess how they are going to vote."

    He said he had also encountered a lot of Labour voters who were "disenchanted" with the party and the direction it was going in under Jeremy Corbyn's leadership.

    And they felt "ignored" by Ms Hardy, who supports a second referendum and has voted against the Brexit deals that have been put before parliament by Theresa May and Boris Johnson.

    However, he acknowledged: "A lot of voters are going to us, but a lot are also going to the Brexit Party."

    The Brexit Party is indeed serious about winning here too.

    Frontman Nigel Farage has already been up to Hull last week to host his big campaign in the north; and is due to visit again this week.

    The party also took the decision to of dump its original candidate in favour of Michelle Dewberry, the former Apprentice star and Hull businesswoman who stood as an independent in the last general election.

    And in Ms Dewberry, the Brexit Party has selected a recognisable and local candidate to take on Ms Hardy.

    If it had selected a less well-known name, Ms Hardy may have had less reason to worry.

    Labour's fight to hold on

    Ms Hardy, a former teacher and organiser for the teachers' union won Hull West and Hessle in the 2017 general election, making her a relatively new intake to parliament.

    She in fact increased the number of votes that went to Mr Johnson, a Labour heavyweight and former home secretary, education secretary and health secretary under Tony Blair and Gordon Brown, from 15,646 to 18,342.

    People close to Ms Hardy acknowledge that there has already been an inevitable backlash for Leave voters on the doorstep, but say it was to be expected.

    They are counting on Ms Hardy's respected record as a local MP, in which she has campaigned to cut the cost of school uniforms, the removal of isolation booths in schools and on the vaginal mesh scandal.

    Ms Hardy is standing firm in her support of the Labour party's policy, which is to renegotiate a new deal with the EU - with some form of customs union and close alignment to the single market - and put it back to the public in a second referendum alongside the option to Remain.

    And then there is the presence of the Liberal Democrats and their unequivocal stance to remain in the EU. The Lib Dems have performed well locally in Hull, controlling the council until 2011.

    They will be hoping to pick up votes from Remainers in the Tory and Labour parties.

    The key to power

    But the biggest threat to Ms Hardy are undoubtedly the Brexit-supporting parties. Ironically, though, decisions taken at the national level might help her to win.

    The Brexit party's decision not to stand in the 317 seats the Tories won at the last election but to "take the fight to Labour" by fielding candidates in its areas has raised eyebrows and attracted criticism.

    The reasoning behind the move was to pave the way for a Tory majority, but if that were the aim, the argument goes that the Brexit Party should be standing aside in Tory target seats too - of which Hull West and Hessle is a prime example.

    Joe Twyman, the co-founder and director of Deltapoll, told Hull Live that the the Tories have to hope that the Brexit Party acts as a "gateway drug".

    "The Tories have to hope that Labour votes go down, but what they really need is for people to go to them," he said.

    "The Brexit party is very unlikely to win an overall majority, so who is it that they're helping?

    "Hull West and Hessle is the type of seat that the Tories need to pick up if they are to win a majority. If they can't pick up leave-voting Labour seats, it is difficult to see where that majority comes from."

    Brexit Party candidate Michelle Dewberry does not believe people in Hull will support the Conservative Party.

    "Ultimately the facts speak for themselves," she said.

    "The reality is that Hull West and Hessle has not once been Tory, the Tories have never once come close and I do not believe that people have it within them to vote for the Tories.

    "A vote for the Tories is just going to return Labour."

    Hull West and Hessle voting history

    2017 general election

    Labour: 18,342

    Conservatives: 10,317

    Liberal Democrats: 2,210

    2016 EU referendum

    Leave: 67.8 per cent

    Remain: 32.2 per cent

    2015 general election

    Labour: 15,646

    Ukip: 6,313

    Conservatives: 5, 561
