How to cut cable’s cord and go streaming in 2019

ByDwight Silverman,Staff writerUpdated Nov 8, 2019 11:11 a.m.

Dropping cable TV and watching television and movies via streaming is more popular then ever, but that doesn’t mean it’s easy. By canceling cable, you give up its organization and ease of finding what you want to watch. The main reason to become a cord cutter is that, if you do it right, it costs less.

But as the streaming TV landscape evolves, it’s less of a bargain. And that’s going to be even more true starting next year.

When I first dropped cable TV in 2013, I went from paying about $1,400 annually to $588. It was simpler back then - I bought season passes for shows I liked via Apple’s iTunes and Amazon Video, had a Netflix subscription . . . and that was about it.

That was before the current Great Upheaval™, in which the creators of TV shows and movies are reeling in their content from the license agreements that allowed aggregators sucha as Netflix and Hulu to become one-stop-shops for viewing. Companies such as CBS, NBC/Universal, TimeWarner, Disney and others have realized the cable TV model is withering away and streaming is the future. They’re building their own streaming services.

That means you’re going to have to make some choices. Subscribing to everything is going to drop you in the same expensive proposition that drove you to cancel cable. With that in mind, here’s my cord-cutting guide for 2019 and 2020. Some of this advice is the same as last year’s, but much of it has been overhauled.

Step 1: Audit your watching

Begin by paying attention to - and even writing down - what you watch. This process will let you figure out how much money you can save, as well as whether cord-cutting is even for you. If you have kids in your household, this will also let you get a handle on just how much TV they are watching.

Most people watch a fixed number of TV shows a year. Put into a spreadsheet their names, networks or channels. . (Don't include sports. We'll get to that in a minute.) Leave a blank field, because that's where we'll put the price of subscribing to them.

One of the biggest mistakes newbie cord cutters make is trying to duplicate the cable TV channel bundle. My advice is: Don't. Instead, think in terms of getting access to specific shows, not channels.

Next, go to your favorite source for streaming movies and TV shows — such as iTunes or Amazon Prime Video — and look up the prices for a season of each show. Typically, they'll run $25-$35 per season.

Now put those numbers into the empty field you left on your spreadsheet. Add a line for Netflix, Hulu or whatever streaming service you think you might use regularly. Multiple the monthly cost for that by 12.

(If you want to use Amazon Prime, remember that its $119-per-year cost also includes other services, not the least of which is free shipping. Calculating the exact cost for the video feature is difficult, but you can probably apply half the annual cost in your spreadsheet.)

Add all these numbers and then divide by 12. If that number is less than the cost of your cable TV bill minus internet, then cord cutting makes sense for you.

That is . . . unless you're a big watcher of sports. While watching sports without cable is somewhat easier now, cable companies still have a lock on local professional teams. For example, you must have AT&T SportsNet in the Houston area to watch the Rockets or the Astros during their regular seasons. The NBA and MLB streaming services black out local teams. (An exception is playoff games, in which local teams can be found on streamable cable channels.)

Step 2: Test your internet

Until recently, "cord cutting" was a misnomer, because internet services are often supplied by the same wire-based operation that provides TV. However you get online, it must be fast enough to handle video — and multiple video streams if more than one person wants to watch at a time.

Now, though, at least one wireless internet service is designed to deliver broadband-level speeds at a good price, and more are coming soon. Verizon’s 5G Home uses next-generation wireless technology to deliver up to 1-gigabit-per-second speeds. But it’s not available everywhere in Houston, and even if it is in your neighborhood, you may not be able to receive its signal, which is easily blocked by trees and other impediments. If you can, though, it rivals the fastest speed tiers available from Comcast and AT&T (and even those aren’t available everywhere).

Generally, the minimum speed for being able to stream HD video is 5 megabits a second, according to Netflix. If you want to stream higher-resolution video - such as 4K or Ultra-High Definition - you’ll need at least 25. And if you have more than one stream going at a time, 50-100 Mbps is a good idea. In a big household with lots of watchers, think about 100 Mbps or faster. Think there may be some online gaming going on as well as video watching? Consider 150 Mbps or more.

If you don't know how fast your internet connection is, use a computer either connected directly to your home router via Ethernet, or via Wi-Fi in the same room as the router, and visit SpeedTest.net. Run several tests to get an average. If your speed is below the guidelines detailed above, you may need an upgrade.

Step 3: Consider your hardware

If you're dropping cable, that means you'll lose your cable box, but depending on the TV you own, you might want to buy a different type of hardware to replace it.

Streaming TV uses apps either installed on the set itself or on what's known as an over-the-top box, such as Apple TV, Amazon's Fire TV, Google's Chromecast or Roku. These boxes connect either via Ethernet cable or Wi-Fi to your home network's router.

If you have a smart TV that has the software you need, you may not need to buy anything else. But if your TV set is older, it may not have this capability. And even if your older set does have its own apps, they may be out of date and are not able to be updated. In that case, you'll want to buy an over-the-top box.

Which box should you buy if you need one? If you've already got Apple products in your home, such as a Mac or an iPhone, you may want to consider Apple TV, which can work in conjunction with them. If you've got an Android phone, you may want to use Chromecast. And if you've got an Amazon Echo, the Fire TV or smaller Fire Stick may be the way to go.

With the exception of the Chromecast, each of these boxes have apps that let you watch the channels and shows you want. Chromecast lets you run apps on your phone that you then "cast" to the TV.

If you’re a Comcast customer, you may also want to consider Xfinity Flex, an over-the-top streaming box from the provider. It’s available free to internet-only customers, and has an interface similar to that of Comcast’s X1 cable box. It has one big downside: It doesn’t support any of the cable-bundle replacement services, such as SlingTV or YouTube TV.

Step 4: Choose your sources

This is the trickiest part, and the one that can cost you almost as much as your cable bill if you are not careful.

Several streaming-only packages give you access to many cable channels by paying a single amount — just like traditional cable TV. If you're a channel surfer, this is the way to go. They also are a good way to provide sports programming, though you may or may not be able to see local teams. However, they are also the most pricey option, and extras can quickly get you closer to the price of “real” cable.

There's Google's YouTube TV ($50 monthly); Sling TV ($15-25 monthly, plus many "extras" for extra coast); AT&T TV Now ($50-$70 monthly, plus add-ons); FuboTV, which has lots of sports programming ($55-$80 monthly).

Then there's Hulu ($6-$45 monthly, plus add-ons) or Netflix ($9-$16 monthly), with original programming, familiar network and cable shows and movies. Amazon Prime Video is similar and is included with the cost of the $119 Amazon Prime. If you order enough items with Prime's free shipping from the online retailer, it pays for itself.

Apple TV+ recently launched with nine shows and a movie, but no back catalog of content like Netflix or Hulu offer. It’s $5 a month, but if you recently bought an iPhone, iPad, Macintosh computer or the Apple TV streaming box, it’s free for a year. Apple TV+ is available on all Apple devices, as well as Amazon Fire TV, Roku and newer LG and Samsung TVs. It’s also included in the YouTube TV subscription. It can also be watched on the web at tv.apple.com.

Some individual channels have their own freestanding streaming options that don't require a cable login. ESPN, HBO, Showtime, CBS and many others are available for a smaller monthly fee, ranging from $10-$15. Sports networks also are available — MLB, NFL, NBA, etc. — with fairly expensive packages that typically black out local games.

And, of course there is a lot more coming. Disney+ ($7 monthly, $70 annually), launches Nov. 12. It will work on Apple TV, iOS devices, Android devices, Roku, Xbox One, PlayStation 4 and Amazon’s FireTV. It include Disney’s deep bench of content, including Marvel, Star Wars, Pixar and Disney’s catalog of movies and TV shows.

Next year, heavyweights such as TimeWarner, HBO, NBC enter the streaming market. Pricing varies, but if you want to watch them all, it’s going to cost you.

But here’s the good news: These streaming packages don't come with contracts. You can subscribe to one and drop it as needed. For example, if you're a fan of HBO’s “Barry”, for example, you can subscribe to HBO Now (and soon HBO Max) for the weeks that it airs, then cancel the service when the season is done. And many have a short, free trial period. Try one and if it doesn't suit your needs, sample another.

You can also buy an antenna that lets you pick up digital, over-the-air signals. These antennas can cost as little as $25 and allow you to pick up dozens of channels in the Houston area, though how many you receive will depend on your location in relation to the station's transmitter.

Finally, as I mentioned earlier, you can use Apple's iTunes or Amazon Prime Video to buy season passes of shows. An added bonus: The episodes are all commercial-free. A combination of purchased seasons and either Hulu, Netflix and an old-school antenna will give you more than enough entertainment.

Step 5: Cut your cord

This is the final step.

If you need to change internet providers, do that first, and arrange for service to be turned on a day or two before you drop cable TV and your previous internet provider. If you are keeping your current internet service, make sure it's working well and is fast enough (see Step 2).

When you call to cancel your TV service, be prepared to hit resistance. The representative will try to talk you out of it, offer you a lower rate (possibly with fewer features or channels) and likely pass you off to a retention specialist in an attempt to keep you as a customer. If you're determined to drop cable TV, be strong. You'll hear a lot of promises and deals that sound good. But if you've done your homework, you'll know they're not as good as not having cable TV.

Once the rep gives up, you'll be told where to bring your cable equipment to return it. You may be offered a credit for that part of the month you didn't use.

At that point, you've made it. You're now cable TV-free. Enjoy your savings, but don't expect to have more free time. There's a LOT of television out there to watch.
