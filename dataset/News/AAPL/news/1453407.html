Apple And Health: An Upcoming Launch?

Enrique Dans

Let me get this out of the way first: no, Apple has not announced the launch of any new health-related product lately. But an interesting article by Rob Litterst and Nathan Baschez, entitled “HealthOS: Apple is building an operating system for health”, touches on something I have been convinced about for some time, and which I have even used as an exam topic in some of my recent courses on innovation: the possibility that Apple, at some point, will decide to integrate its many initiatives in the field of health into some type of monitoring service.

Apple has long been obsessed with health, and Tim Cook has gone so far as to say that this could be the field in which the company could make its greatest contribution to humanity. The elements of this growing orientation toward health seem increasingly clear: buying companies focused on the safe recording of medical information, hiring doctors to work in its health laboratories, developing its own FDA-approved health products, acquiring other products from third parties, working to incorporate sensors for important parameters such as oxygen saturation (which could help detect COVID-19) and blood sugar, or incorporating sensors in AirPods, along with the launch of initiatives such as ResearchKit and CareKit, together with ambitious studies with institutions such as Johnson & Johnson and Stanford Medical.

At the same time, Apple’s increasing focus on services suggests that a position such as the existing one of aggregating information from multiple suppliers and devices around Apple Health, which today already collects sensor readings from its own devices and any other third party device or app that wishes to join, could make a lot of sense and be very interesting in terms of three very important issues: the reduction in patient suffering due to earlier detection of diseases and illnesses, reducing the cost of treatment, and the possibility of carrying out medical research with a hyper-abundance of information that could boost data availability by several orders of magnitude.

The possibility that a company like Apple, with its commitment to data security and privacy, could end up offering a health monitoring service that includes algorithmic alerts base don readings of key parameters and, possibly, in conjunction with insurance companies is, given the current situation, increasingly possible — or even probable.

If I were a betting man, I’d lay a wager that we can expect to see the launch of something along these lines soon.
