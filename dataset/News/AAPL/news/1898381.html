Effects of Brexit and COVID-19 could lead to farming crisis in Cornwall

Campaign launches to attract local manual workers into agricultural employment

    Cornwall requires around 4,500 manual workers to ensure its seasonal crops – and, in turn, its farms – survive during the coronavirus pandemic.

    A mixture of the effects of Brexit and the COVID-19 crisis has led to a lack of overseas migrant workers on Cornish farms, which has caused concern within the agricultural sector.

    Farm Cornwall – a charity which offers “sound, practical, realistic and confidential advice” for those working in farming in the county – has launched a campaign to entice local workers back on farms.

    Edward Richardson, of the Penzance-based charity, told Cornwall Live: “Cornwall requires 4,500 manual workers to not only pick crops but help package and transport them. Across the country the figure needed is around 70,000."

    “The amount of migrant workers in Cornwall has dropped off as many have returned home, with many finding that the drop in value of the pound ensured they were better off working at home in countries like Poland.

    Mr Richardson added: “Brexit had a negative effect on manual farm work. Polish workers, for example, found they could get better paid jobs there. There are efforts being made to encourage them back again by raising pay and improving accommodation.”

    The restriction on travel has also meant a dearth of overseas manual workers in Cornwall.

    He said some agricultural businesses have been able to fly people in during the coronavirus crisis but the 750 manual workers who have arrived in Britain only account for 1% of the required workforce.

    “If coronavirus has stopped people coming in then this a golden opportunity for British people to re-engage with farming or, for those who don’t have a link with farming, to discover how rewarding it is. We really need people in our own country to work on our farms.”

    He used an example a local grower of courgettes who would need 70 people, working in one field, to pick all the crop.

    He stressed that people working on farms would help to avert a crisis.

    “The worst case scenario if the crops aren’t harvested is they will rot in the fields, which will have a knock-on effect and we could see businesses failing.”

    Farm Cornwall is now launching a bid to link people looking for manual work with farmers who find themselves in difficulty.

    Workers are needed to help pick cabbages, cauliflower, potatoes, courgettes and flowers, which are due in late May and early June.

    Farm England has uploaded online details where both prospective employees and farms / producers can register.

    So far, there have been over 100 responses from people keen to work on farms with 40 potential businesses looking for staff.

    “We need a lot more than that and especially for the end of May and beginning of June when the crops will be ready to come out of the ground,” said Mr Richardson.

    Farm Cornwall is working on providing transport to get workers to the various farms across Cornwall.

    Discussions are already taking place between Farm Cornwall and local businesses to ensure workers will be safe if the lockdown is still an issue in May and June, with ways of practically implementing social distancing.

    For more details and to get involved see Farm Cornwall’s website.

    #ComeBackLater

    Devon and Cornwall is understandably proud of its renowned stunning coastlines, wide open spaces and gallons of fresh air and thrive from the millions of visitors who come to enjoy our part of the world each year.

    However, the outbreak of coronavirus in the UK has changed our day to day lives while the government is calling for the nation to stop all non essential travel in a bid to stop the spread of the disease that has so tragically claimed lives in the UK.

    In the South West not only do we have a proportion of elderly people living here, those who are some of the most vulnerable to coronavirus, but we also have NHS trusts that are stretched to capacity without any extra pressure.

    We want to help saves lives and help bring an end to the outbreak as soon as we possibly can.

    Therefore we are aiming to spread the message of come back later as far and as wide as possible through a campaign launching today - #comebacklater.

     

    In other farming news, Cornwall’s Farming Health Hub has produced a new leaflet aimed at signposting people to where they can go for help and advice during the coronavirus pandemic.

    With the lockdown restrictions already reported to have had a significant impact on the sale and distribution of local produce, including milk, and the availability of farm workers to pick seasonal crops, the decision to extend the restrictions for at least a further three weeks has led to growing fears for the physical and mental health and wellbeing of people in farming and rural communities.

    “While people working in the agricultural sector can access some of the business support measures already announced by the Government, we know that many are struggling to understand and access the ever-changing advice and guidance about what might be available to them “ said Jon James, chief executive of the Farming Health Hub.

    “This is adding to the stress and pressures farmers and their families are under which is, in turn, affecting their physical and mental health and wellbeing.”

    He added: “With so many people in rural communities across Cornwall dependent on farming for their income, it is vital that organisations pull together to support and guide the farming community.

    “We have worked with people across the private, public and voluntary sector to develop this document which contains the latest information about the support which is currently available, as well as practical advice on how people can protect their physical and mental health.”

    CornwallLive launches CornwallTogether campaign

    Here at CornwallLive we are feeling the impact of thecoronavirus crisis, as is much of the county.

    Cornwall is an area that is proud of its community spirit and historically has relied on it through all sorts of hardships.

    That's why today CornwallLive has launched our CornwallTogether campaign -- urging all of our readers to work together and look after each other as the region battles to get through the current situation.

    Now, more than ever, we want to tell the positive stories that shine through the darkness of this global pandemic.

    We will be here to highlight the heroes, whether in our NHS or in our local communities, who are going above and beyond to keep people safe.

    We will be here to stand up for the local businesses that need our support.

    We will also, of course, be covering all developments around coronavirus to provide you with the most accurate and up-to-date information and advice from trusted sources to the best of our ability.

    That will include advice and support from some of the most important charities and organisations operating in Cornwall.
     

    Cornwall is a large county and we have an elderly population who live in isolated areas. These, along with independent businesses, are the people who will need us the most in the coming months.

    The Cornwall Together campaign urges our readers to:

    • Support local shops and businesses where possible
    • Look out for their neighbours and friends

    • Look after themselves as increasing numbers of us self isolate and work from home

    We have launched some new services in order to stay in touch with you during these unfamiliar times.

    We will also be sending twice-daily newsletters, as well as breaking news updates, via email.

    Our app, which is available to download free in the App Store, will also offer breaking news alerts and remains the fastest and easiest way to read our articles.

    One of the organisations involved in the creation of the leaflet is the Farming Community Network . Cornwall FCN co-ordinator Sue Gillbard says the network is still taking cases which are being managed over the phone during the crisis.

    “We recognise that this is a very difficult time for the farming community,” she said. “There is lots of useful information on the Farmwell page of our website and we also have a helpline which is open from 7am until 11pm if farmers want someone to talk to.  The number is 03000 111 999.“
