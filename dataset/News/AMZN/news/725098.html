The Perfect Deal: Joe Rogan’s Exclusive Move To Spotify

This deal is YouTube’s loss and Spotify’s gain.

On May 20, 2020, Joe Rogan announced an exclusive, multiyear deal with Spotify. The deal could be worth more than $100 million, according to The Wall Street Journal. The agreement, as described by Spotify, would make the podcast free for all of its 286 million users.

For podcasts, this deal is unprecedented: Rogan will most likely earn more money than most musicians on Spotify.

But the 52-year-old is no ordinary podcaster; he is arguably the Michael Jordan of podcasts.

Rogan started his podcast The Joe Rogan Experiencein 2009. He runs a two-hour long show daily; exploiting YouTube’s algorithm which favors long videos.

His one rule is that he doesn’t follow conventional rules. His podcasts are mostly fun, but as equally controversial. For one, the infamous meme of Elon Musk smoking pot was born in the stage of Rogan’s podcast.

With a staff of four and a microphone, Rogan garners over 190 million downloads per month. Last year, he reportedly made $30 million.

These numbers are quite alluring for advertisers. And perhaps so, after the news broke, Spotify’s stock price surged by nearly 10 percent.

Spotify’s Strategy: Podcast Ambitions & New Competition

For Spotify, signing Rogan is the next step of its core strategic plan for its podcasting ambitions and exclusive deals. Last year, the company made three key acquisitions, reportedly spending around $400 million combined on two podcast networks — Gimlet Media, and Parcast —and one company that offers podcast creation tools, Anchor.

Long before the deal with Rogan, Spotify has been spending big on talent acquisition. In February, Spotify paid up to $196 million in cash to acquire Bill Simmons’ flagship show, The Ringer. It has also committed to exclusive deals with other prominent names, like Amy Schumer and Joe Budden.

Their dream is clear: Spotify wants to blow its competition out of the water. Its main competition? Apple Music, which leads the rank in podcast players. At least it was.

But Spotify has made it clear that it’s planning to expand the competition. Its new competition? YouTube.

In early May, Spotify started testing video podcasts in its app with two Youtube stars: Zane Hijazi and Heath Hussar, hosts of Zane and Heath: Unfiltered.

Rogan’s influence will inspire other podcasters to follow his lead. And Spotify’s recently acquired company, Anchor will help create more shows. Already, Anchor has helped create 70 percent of shows, as per Spotify’s report from the first quarter of 2020. The same report shows that Spotify already hosts more than a million podcasts.

This exclusive deal is YouTube’s loss and Spotify’s gain. And it is not the end of the story, but a new beginning.

A New Spotify in the Making

Spotify has also officially joined the advertising game that might emulate Google’s and Facebook’s programmatic ads: Offer an enticing product for free but in exchange for user data which can be used to sell more lucrative ads.

Although the show will be free on Spotify, people will be paying with their data. If users subscribe for an ad-free experience, the money is in Spotify’s pocket. If they don’t, the ads that will run for Rogan’s show, plus Spotify’s additional ads will ensure a win-win situation for Spotify in either case.

One caveat of following Google and Facebook on ad policy is the privacy controversy, which both companies have faced in recent years.

There is also a recent trend in platforms crossing paths with and against each other. Everyone wants to be able to do everything — like Google.

YouTube and Facebook are planning to invest in live TV — replacing almost redundant traditional cables. Amazon, an E-commerce company, along with Apple, want to stream videos.

All the platforms are now competing for the user’s undivided attention. They don’t want to share it anymore.

The Perfect Deal — For Spotify

It is often said that a crisis, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, provides great business opportunities for those well-prepared. So was Spotify.

With ad and sponsorship revenue at loss due to coronavirus, it was imminent that Rogan would accept Spotify’s lucrative offer to become the face of the company’s podcast ambitions.

Rogan signed an exclusive licensing agreement for a yet undisclosed period of time. This means that he still retains ownership over all his Intellectual Property.

The loss for Rogan is that for an undisclosed amount of time, he has to remove his content from YouTube’s 2 billion users for Spotify, which has just over 10% of YouTube’s total user count.

Spotify has reportedly paid a hefty fee of $100 million upfront, but with Rogan as its face for its podcast services, success is inevitable for the Swedish music service company. The swift rise in its stock price caused soon after the deal’s announcement is only the beginning. The best is yet to come for Spotify with this strategic long term investment.

The Joe Rogan Experience will be available on Spotify starting from September 1, 2020, and from January 2021, exclusive on the platform. Short clips on his short clip YouTube channel will continue to be available on YouTube but the full episodes will not.
