Amazon’s Looming Problem: Who Has The Strategic Mindset To Replace Jeff Bezos?

Let’s say you’re an investor in Amazon. If Jeff Bezos were no longer CEO, are you confident that this e-tailing giant would keep growing at over 20% a year?

I think the answer depends on who takes Bezos’s place. And that depends on the strategic mindset of Amazon’s next CEO. A Head in the Sand CEO who keeps asking ‘What would Jeff do?’ would be disastrous.

However, a CEO with a market-share winning vision of the future — e.g., Create the Future — or the ability to galvanize an organization to execute an existing vision — e.g., Fast Follower mindset — might be great for shareholders.

Since I see them as Fast Followers, if either of Amazon’s top two executives — CEO Worldwide Consumer Jeff Wilke or AWS CEO Andy Jassy — were to succeed Bezos, Amazon shareholders would be in good hands over the short term.

The question is whether either has enough Create the Future in them to enable Amazon to sustain its faster than 20% annual revenue growth rate by investing in new growth opportunities.

Yale’s Jeff Sonnenfeld is more bullish on the pair. As he told me in a June 20 interview, “I don’t know [Wilke and Jassy] personally but I do know Bezos and believe they’ve constantly been reinventing their business...[and they migrated] the company...for reliable execution.”

My interviews with three venture capitalists reveal another opportunity — look to invest in the IPOs of venture capital firms with a track record of backing marathoners— founders who turn an idea into a fast-growing public company, according to Scaling Your Startup.

Three CEO Mindsets And Why They Matter To Investors

Investors in public companies ought to care about the mindset of its CEO. That’s because mindset determines the way a leader pulls the seven levers — such as sustaining culture, building growth trajectories, and hiring, promoting, and letting people go — that determine how fast a company grows.

While these are over-simplified and should not be thought of as type-casting specific CEOs for life, here’s a more detailed description of three CEO strategic mindsets.

  • Create the future. Bezos epitomizes this mindset. He envisions where an industry is heading and gets there ahead of everyone else – thereby building a large company that dominates its markets. If such a CEO continues to reinvent the company, it keeps growing. The biggest challenge for a board of such a company is to groom a successor who can keep the company in the lead after the founder departs.
  • Fast follower.Such a CEO — Microsoft’s Satya Nadella is an example — is good at seeing where the industry is headed and which companies are leading it. Then the Fast Follower develops a strategy that builds on her company’s strengths — enabling it to gain market share by offering customers a more compelling value proposition than many incumbents.
  • Head in the sand.This kind of CEOis locked into a view of the world that could be decades old – one that worked as the individual was rising to the top job. This CEO has a bad case of confirmation bias – and can’t look at where the industry is headed and how the company must adapt to survive. Such CEOs often prevail over the demise of the company. Digital Equipment founder and CEO, Ken Olson, hit this iceberg when he dismissed the importance of the PC and the Internet — changing from a Create the Future mindset to a Head in the Sand one.

Which type of CEO you pick to succeed the founder has enormous consequences for shareholders.

Consider the ginormous blunder Bill Gates made when he handed over the reins of Microsoft to his number two, Steve Ballmer — a Head in the Sand CEO who pushed Windows-first. Then take a look at how much better off Microsoft shareholders are now that Ballmer has been replaced by Satya Nadella.

In his 14 years at Microsoft’s helm, the stock fell at a 2.2% annual rate whereas Nadella — a Fast Follower who changed Microsoft’s culture and built its cloud business into a formidable challenger to AWS — presided over a 29% average annual increase in its stock — leaving the shares nearly four times higher than where they had previously peaked under Bill Gates.

To be fair to Ballmer, Microsoft revenue between 1999 and 2013 grew at an average annual rate of 9.8% to $77.8 billion; whereas Nadella presided over 8.3% annual top line growth through 2019 to $126 billion.

If you are an Apple shareholder, are you happy that Steve Jobs appointed Tim Cook — more of a Fast Follower type — as his successor? Apple is a far less innovative company than it was under Jobs. Yet Cook has presided over the introduction of the Apple Watch, extended the life of the iPhone, and attempted to build services as a bigger source of revenue. Sadly, Apple’s overall revenues have plateaued.

Jobs’s Create the Future mindset has worked better for shareholders than Cook’s Fast Follower one. Apple’s revenue in 1997 was $7.1 billion, growing at a 21.4% average annual rate in the 14 years through 2011. Between 2011 and 2019, Apple’s revenue grew at an 11.6% annual rate from $108 billion to $260 billion.

Jobs also aced his successor when it came to stock price growth. After Jobs returned as CEO in July 1997, its stock price grew faster — up an average 39.5% in the nearly 14 years before he handed the reins over to Cook — who presided over a 22.8% annual stock price growth since Jobs stepped down in August 2011.

Developing a Create the Future CEO Who Can Scale To IPO and Grow a Public Company

One compelling way for investors to bet on the right CEO is to buy shares in the IPO of a company that has been led from its inception by a founder who develops the skills to run a public company and keep it growing fast. Examples of such CEOs include well-known names like Bezos, Gates, and Facebook’s Mark Zuckerberg.

Others whom you may not have heard of are Eric Yuan, CEO of Zoom Video and Peter Gassner, founder and CEO of Veeva Systems. Both have proven to be adept at growing revenue, taking their companies public, and boosting their stock price.

Yuan’s leadership — about which I wrote here and here — has benefited Zoom’s employees, customers, and shareholders. Between 2017 and 2020, Zoom’s revenues grew at a 117% annual rate from about $61 million to $623 million. From its April 2019 IPO to June 25, 2020, Zoom shares soared at a 198% annual rate to $258.

Gassner has also done an excellent job shifting from founder to public company CEO. Between 2012 and 2020, Veeva’s revenues grew at a 43.6% annual rate from about $61 million to $1,104 million. From its October 2013 IPO to June 25, 2020, Veeva shares rose at a 29% annual rate to $236.

You could do worse than keeping an eye on companies taken public by Emergence Capital Partners. As its general partner, Santi Subotovsky, explained in a June 23 interview, “When we assess CEOs and leaders in general we work hard to get to the bottom of their motivations as we find a high correlation between this and success. We also have the advantage of having seen leaders like Yuan and Gassner early in their journeys.”

Emergence looks for CEOs who have unique personalities. “Leaders building iconic companies are typically Irrationally obsessed; they have a strong conviction about how the future should work, and they don't stop until their visions become a reality. These CEOs will work intensely to make this happen and as a result they will weather the storms that are part of the company building journeys,” noted Subotovsky.

Chris Lynch, a startup investor and current CEO of data virtualization provider AtScale sees hunger and competitive drive as among the critical traits for a founder who can grow from an idea to taking a company public.

As he told me in a June 23 interview, “Fewer than 50% of company founders are still CEO when the company goes public. I have one company, AI platform, DataRobot, that has gone from $0 to $100 million in revenue with the original CEO. He is impatient with stupid questions so he would not like talking to a hedge fund analyst who owns the company’s stock. He has technical chops and vision. And he has [grit] after overcoming poverty and other challenges. He is self aware and intensely competitive. He has leadership skills. He knows how to motivate engineering, sales and marketing. He has the x-factor: blind ambition. He wants to build the next Google and is willing to sacrifice his personal life to get there,” said Lynch.

Kara Nortman, Partner at Upfront Ventures, likes to back founders who can take a company public and keep it growing. As she said in a June 25 interview, “As early stage investors, our starting point is always to back the forever CEO and then we evolve with the company over time.”

One Upfront company that got part of the way there is Fleetsmith — a service that cuts the time to make a worker’s iPhone or other device usable on a company’s systems — which Apple acquired on June 24. This deal makes it clear that Fleetsmith CEO Zack Blum will have to look to a future company to prove that he has what it takes to be a forever CEO.

Nortman liked Fleetsmith’s vision and what she calls its management team’s earned insight. “[I found compelling Fleetsmith’s] vision of zero trust deployment. They slash the four hours that an employee takes to get their new device [working with their employer’s systems.] Their vision was to apply this seamless process to almost anything. Their top people had earned insight. One was in charge of server security for Dropbox; another ran IT [for a company], and a third is a strong, scalable chief technology officer.”

Find Venture Capital Firms Who Back Leaders of Fast Growing Post-IPO Companies

Given Emergence’s track record, I’d devote extra time to analyze whether to buy into the IPO of an Emergence-backed startup. Its venture capitalists pick between five and seven companies a year whose founding teams they believe will defy the odds by taking their companies public and staying on to preside over their rapid growth.

The importance of Emergence’s capital investment is dwarfed by the time it invests in the development of its executive teams. “With companies such as Zoom, Veeva, Yammer and Box, we really strive to help beyond just the founding teams, as we partner and support the executives and leaders across go-to-market, product and other functions,” he said.

Emergence uses it board seats to help companies succeed. “As board members, both in public and private companies, we spend a lot of time making sure the executive team gets the support they need to grow with their business. There are formal processes, succession planning as a contingency plan for every key role, as well as more informal one on one meetings to ensure all people are operating at their best. It is very helpful for us, as a venture capital firm focused 100% on the future of work, to see the index and share those insights with the teams we work with,” Subotovsky concluded.

Here are two implications for investors:

  1. As long as Bezos is Amazon’s CEO, I am bullish on its shares. But the uncertainty about Amazon’s succession plan is a concern.
  2. I would seriously consider investing in any Emergence-backed company that files for an IPO.
