Working To Source And Secure Affordable PPE For Small Businesses

ByMoira VetterFormer Contributor
I write about how entrepreneurs raise capital and manage money.
Jun 12, 2020, 12:40pm EDT
This article is more than 4 years old.

Business owners have long been the "chief cook and bottle washers." We can now, thanks to Covid-19, add to that list of duties "securer of PPE."

With the government, large health care systems and other essential public service businesses Hoovering up much of our nation's supply, small business is left with fewer and higher-priced options to meet for their PPE needs.

Although the Department of Justice formed a Covid-19 Hoarding and Price Gouging Task Force and is operating under an Executive Order to prevent predatory practices, we all know commerce moves faster than the government. Additionally, the Secretary of HHS issued a notice to ensure people understand the items that must remain freely available, including the masks, shields, gloves, and disinfecting devices and products that small businesses need upon reopening.

And this is where public/private partnerships are critical. It is one thing to provide guidance on what should be available under free-market conditions. It is another thing to work as a collective to create a market of reasonably priced products that can be found quickly and efficiently by businesses that do not have a corporate procurement function.

The group-buying model is nothing new, but it is being newly applied to PPE. I remember in the 1990s, healthcare costs were skyrocketing, and small employers struggled to offer competitive health benefits. Many small businesses joined large organizations—similar to the way PEOs work today—primarily for the cost-effectiveness of group health insurance these associations made available to their members. The American Electronics Association (now TechAmerica/CompTIA), was one such organization that made it possible for many tech startups to access resources they couldn't get on their own.

Now, Chambers of Commerce and other organizations across the country are working to ensure that their member companies have access to the supplies and products they will need to open up safely and soon.

For example, in Oregon, you can access a list of trusted suppliers of a variety of PPE. Here in Georgia, a site provides an interactive list and map to help you find suppliers by category.

You want to trust your suppliers, and you want to understand the continuity of supply, so you don't have to reinvent the wheel every time you need "one of those" or "some of that stuff." If you have to go to one of 15 places to get all the things you need, you could eventually become a professional procurement manager and not a leader focused on your business's core competency.

It was the "one place to safely shop and get good prices," which was the basis for the Amazon model in the first place. Well, no, in the first place, it was positioned as a bookstore, but I don't have time to go down the Amazon rabbit hole.

One collective of Carolina Chambers of Commerce (including Greater Chapel Hill-Corrboro, Greater Durham, Triangle East, and others) has partnered with New Day Products to make PPE available to their members. Through their website ChamberPPE.com, member companies can shop directly and apply a discount code for purchases. According to a release by the Greater Chapin Chamber of Commerce, "This website provides the option of purchasing items individually, in bulk, or in kits based upon your specific needs. The pricing on these items is generally 20% - 50% below competitor's pricing based on the quantity business orders."

I compared some of the products to see what a small business owner with 5 minutes and Amazon might get, rather than using a site like this. Not surprisingly, the products most in demand have more providers and a greater range of price points.

  • Disposable Masks – These items are not unlike toilet paper. Everyone wants and needs them, and the fear of not securing them makes the costs irrelevant to some. According to the ChamberPPE.com site featuring New Day Products, a 50-pack of 3-ply masks is $36.50 (and then further discounted for members.) If the price is your only deciding factor, Amazon appears to have one supplier of 50-count 3-ply masks that sells for $3.33 with $6.99 shipping. But cost alone should not be your only purchase criteria. It is essential to read about how and where the materials are sourced and when they may be delivered. Many of us have placed orders only to receive notices that our products would be shipped in a month or more given current demand—after we've made our payments.
  • N95 Masks– These masks have been in demand and experienced complicated supply dynamics given their use by healthcare and essential government services. N95 masks have also fallen prey early in the pandemic to counterfeit providers that called into question the quality and safety of products being purchased. These purchases are confusing for anyone. If you go to Amazon and don't pay attention, you'll see a variety of masks, many of which are not N95s feeding into your search. You may not scroll down to see the message that N95 masks are prioritized for Government and Healthcare and "currently unavailable." I checked eBay too, and you can no longer get steady—or perhaps reputable—supply there either. According to the ChamberPPE.com site, you can buy a 5-pack of N95 masks for $17.25 and your additional member discount.
  • Sanitizer Liquid in 1 Gallon– Sanitizer demand has sparked an entire cottage industry, and not unlike craft beer, many are opting to make their own. For a small business owner with no time for that, you can get gallon jug for $42.50. If you go to other sites, you'll notice variances on the concentration of alcohol (for example, 70%, 80%) for $44 to $50 a gallon. Volume purchases can get you a better price, but it is important not to overstock, just for the presumption of savings. And again, to state the obvious, who is making this stuff? If like the Carolina Chambers, you have partnered with a company you know and trust, your peace of mind and safety go way up. In the black hole of Amazon, do you really know who is making and bottling this chemical, and how? Price gouging is a real thing. Anecdotally, I recently spoke to an engineering firm that was pressured in a panic to buy a $1,500 palette of sanitizer to cover their entire manufacturing and shop environment for the next quarter. They felt much better until the invoice came for $15,000. When we talked about it, we laughed, because that's all you can do once the crying is over.

I'm not suggesting that buying a few hundred dollars of PPE is going to be the thing that breaks the bank. But PPE items, like every business purchase, need to be considered for cost, quality, and safety. And, let's be honest, you're not getting out of this for a few hundred bucks in purchases. My company is already a few thousand dollars into stocking up, and we're not even back in the office yet.

Take a look at your local Chamber, and the associations or local business collectives in your area. Have they put together a comprehensive program to do the hard work of vetting quality suppliers that won't gouge you?

If you find a good source, don't take a hoarder's mentality and keep it to yourself. Share it with your colleagues so that all of us opening our businesses—who are undeniably interconnected—can afford to open and stay in business with you.
