RUH medical director shares how Bath hospital is coping with the coronavirus

Dr Bernie Marden discusses preparation, PPE supplies and staff well-being

    Dr Bernie Marden, the Royal United Hospital Baths medical director has said it is business as usual at the hospital despite the coronavirus crisis.

    Although COVID-19 is a new disease, the hospital has responded as it would to any other crisis and is telling patients that nobody should be afraid to go to hospital when they need to.

    Dr Marden says that the hospital has a plentiful supply of personal protection equipment (PPE) and he claims that staff are not being disproportionately infected with the coronavirus.

    In other news, the RUH has changed its visitation policies so that coronavirus patients will no longer face dying alone.

    Dr Marden has worked in the National Health Service for around 27 years, much of it as an acute doctor, and says they are trained to deal with situations like this.

    He said: The health service is incredibly adaptable and resilient in many circumstances.

    We are well trained and have mechanisms to help us deal with uncertainty.

    Its a new disease and we are learning all the time.

    It does certainly test everybody, but the processes are the same regardless of what the emergency is.

    Dr Marden says the RUH has seen a steady increase in coronavirus admissions over the past few weeks.

    He said: At the RUH we have been involved with COVID for a number of weeks now so what we are seeing is normalisation.

    We have been really on the front foot about preparing the hospital and getting ready to look after our COVID patients as best we can."

    The RUH is watching what is happening in London very carefully and is still expecting a peak here but cant say when it will be.

    None of us know precisely what will happen next, Dr Marden said.

    Any hope we have of reducing the peak is directly related to everyone following the social distancing measures.

    But we need to be ready and we are.

    The RUH has also been involved in discussions around Bristols temporary Nightingale hospital where coronavirus patients could be sent if necessary.

    We find it extremely useful to know that we have that additional capacity, Dr Marden said.

    Dr Marden is eager to stress that non-coronavirus patients should still seek the help they need.

    We are still very much open for business as usual, he said.

    People are still having accidents, strokes, heart attacks and so on. We have organised the hospital in a way that makes us confident we can still provide the same level of care for all our patients.

    We are still a very much a functioning, active general hospital providing all the services that people expect us to provide at any hour of the day or night.

    The public is advised to follow the same processes using the emergency number 999, the non-emergency number 111 and pharmacists in the usual way.

    The hospital has set up a separate entrance for suspected coronavirus cases who are treated in isolation away from other patients.

    Nobody should be worried about coming here if they need to, Dr Marden stressed.

    Although there have been stories about a shortage of personal protection equipment (PPE) such as face masks and gloves, Dr Marden says the RUH is well stocked.

    We have good stocks of PPE at the moment and we have been working very closely with others to make sure that remains secure.

    We are so grateful to other organisations who have been supporting us, such as the University of Bath.

    Engineers at the university have now made over 5,500 face shields for hospital staff.

    Dr Marden would not tell us how many staff members had been infected with coronavirus, citing employee and patient confidentiality, but claimed they were not disproportionately affected.

    We have to remember our staff are also members of the public. What we are seeing reflects what we are seeing in the general population, he said.

    He said staff had appropriate protection for dealing with coronavirus patients. He said there were very good systems in place to monitor staff wellbeing and all testing was being done in line with government guidelines.

    Dr Marden says he has been impressed with how all RUH staff have risen to the challenge of the coronavirus.

    He said: What has been overwhelming is the way in which our staff have responded. Whatever their role is in the organisation, this has affected everybody.

    Our teams are finding completely new and innovative ways to help patients such as video consultations and so on.

    The level of flexibility and resilience has been impressive.

    They have also really been there for each other.

    Often we forget those behind the scenes such as cleaners and porters but they are also rising to occasion, taking on different roles and being flexible.

    It brings into sharp focus the values that we hold here, which everyone is living in abundance at the moment.

    The overall message remains the same continue to resist the temptation to go out this long weekend.

    Dr Marden said: The importance of the social distancing measures cannot be overestimated.

    If you stay at home, that directly translates into lives saved.

    The public has shown us huge support in many ways throughout this crisis.

    But staying at home is the single most important way that they can show us support and people have done that really well so far.
