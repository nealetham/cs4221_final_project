Amazon Prime Delivery Is Disrupting Whole Foods (And Not In A Good Way)

Tom Ryan
Contributor
RetailWire
Contributor Group
Opinions expressed by Forbes Contributors are their own.

Amazon AMZN Prime delivery shoppers are reportedly crowding aisles, emptying shelves, ignoring safety rules and bothering busy associates as they scurry around Whole Foods WFM stores fulfilling online orders.

That’s according to a report from Business Insider based on interviews with seven Whole Foods employees, including three managers, across six states.

A manager at one location called Prime workers “vultures” who “come in and pick every department clean.” The first arrive at 6:00 a.m., well before the 8:00 a.m. opening.

Prime shoppers are also accused of often staring down into their phones, neglecting social distancing protocols and one-way aisle rules in their rush to compile orders.

Finally, Prime’s pick and packers were found to be continually pestering Whole Foods’ store staff with questions on item locations. A particular nuisance cited was Amazon.com’s policy of making delivery workers scan a QR code belonging to the Whole Foods associate if an item is out of stock. The stressors for Whole Foods’ associates are said to be exacerbated by under-staffing problems.

"It does strain the Whole Foods store format," wrote Michael Terpkosh, president of City Square Partners, in an online discussion on RetailWirelast week. "Whole Foods aisles are already somewhat narrow and this does not help shoppers trying to navigate the stores. I have also noticed fewer cashiers when there are more pick and pack shopper. This causes shoppers to wait longer to check out. In-store experience is definitely suffering."

Other experts on the RetailWire BrainTrust noted that other chains are struggling with the e-grocery explosion as well. 

"It’s not just Amazon and Whole Foods," wrote Gary Sankary, retail industry strategy at Esri. "On a recent weekday morning trip to my local Target I noticed the ratio of pickers to shoppers was about 1:1, which always makes me question — when the picker and I are both reaching for the last jar of peanut butter, who wins that contest?"

"Shoppers in-store must now navigate big carts roving down the aisles and pickers who have little regard except for their own tasks," wrote Michael La Kier, principal at What Brands Want. "It’s similar to shopping during re-stocking hours: shoppers understand, but it just gets in the way," 

Internal pick and pack personnel — as well as those from third-party delivery services such as Instacart — may become an even more common sight in aisles as households have grown more comfortable with online grocery delivery, BOPIS and curbside pickup during COVID-19. A recent study from Mercatus and Incisv predicted online grocery sales in the U.S. will grow more than 60 percent from pre-pandemic levels to 21.5 percent of sales by 2025.

One BrainTrust member cautioned, though, that the current condition could be better than the alternative.

"Let’s not misplace the blame here," wrote retail expert Mohamed Amer. "Internal and third-party pick and pack personnel reflect an actual shopper that would have been in the store anyways but has chosen to order online. In actuality, these personnel reduce the potential number of physical shoppers that could have been in those aisles since they are picking multiple orders."

Amazon said it expanded its grocery delivery capacity by more than 160 percent and tripled its number of grocery pickup sites. An Amazon spokesperson to Business Insider cited Amazon’s tests of “dark” stores, including the first Whole Foods-supported one’s recent opening in Brooklyn, among other steps being taken.

For Mr. Amer and others, the latter move looked like a critical part of the equation to address this industry-wide issue.

"Grocery retailers need to rethink their stores and consider dark stores, access to warehouses, and so on," wrote Mr. Amer.

"[Shopping] can get pretty sketchy when there are several order pickers on the floor and it’s 5 p.m. and the store is crowded," wrote Richard Hernandez, director at Affiliated Foods. "This is why dark stores would be a perfect solution for the increased online/curbside options."
