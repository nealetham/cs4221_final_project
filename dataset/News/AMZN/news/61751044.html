Prepositions In A Pandemic: How Duolingo Supported A 101% Increase In Learners During COVID-19

It’s no secret that the COVID-19 pandemic has impacted every aspect of our lives. And from an increase in remote workforces to millions of children learning from home, technology is becoming more integral to our success and engagement.

In March 2020, language-learning platform Duolingo saw a 101 percent increase in new users, leading to the highest amount of traffic it had ever seen. For a data-driven company like Duolingo, this growth meant a huge opportunity to better understand customers and app performance. This unprecedented spike could have easily overwhelmed the team responsible for collecting and analyzing user data. Luckily, the company was prepared to scale, using data and analytics solutions from Amazon Web Services (AWS).

A Data-Driven Approach to Language Learning

“Duolingo strives to make high-quality education universally accessible,” says staff software engineer Jonathan Burket. And the company is succeeding—in 2019 its mobile platform was the most downloaded education app in the world. Today, more than 300 million learners across 195 countries use Duolingo to learn 38 languages. And data from a company study indicates that Duolingo learners obtained the equivalent of four semesters of college-level education in nearly half the time.

Burket attributes the company’s success to its data-driven mindset—by using its data to discover insights and by consistently paying attention to its learners and its platform, Duolingo can iterate on certain concerns and execute necessary changes. The company conducts hundreds of A/B tests at any given time, experimenting with new ways of teaching language content. Analytics helps Duolingo measure whether these changes are effective. For example, if the company implements a new feature, it can analyze data to see how learners performed after the change. The company also uses data to find places in lessons where learners get confused or stuck so that it can adjust the curriculum accordingly.

“We can use this data to see how well people are learning and improve a lot of the ways we teach,” Burket explains. “And as part of our effort to make language learning accessible, it’s also important to us that the app itself performs well and provides a pleasant user experience across all devices.”

Modernizing Duolingo’s Analytics Processes

Duolingo once relied on third-party platforms to gain important insights into its learners. But as the company expanded, these services didn’t provide the level of raw data visibility that it needed and were not cost effective, relying on inflexible payment models. Plus, the infrastructure relied on third-party analytics that couldn’t scale to meet user demand—posing a risk to the rapidly growing platform.

Duolingo had built its platform on AWS from the beginning, so the decision to use the cloud data warehouse Amazon Redshift to build its new data architecture was simple. Plus, the similarity between Amazon Redshift’s interface and the SQL databases that company engineers were already familiar with facilitated a relatively streamlined migration. The result was a comprehensive infrastructure that could scale on demand and perform granular data analysis.

“Amazon Redshift was easy to interoperate with a lot of our existing systems,” Burket says. “Having everything under the same umbrella was a big advantage.”

Amazon Redshift provided the foundation for the internal analytics architecture, and then Duolingo could add services to meet specific needs. The company used multiple services to optimize its internal analytics system for various use cases, relying on tools like Amazon Kinesis Data Streams and Amazon Kinesis Data Firehose, which were especially beneficial. Duolingo uses Amazon Kinesis Data Streams to collect, store, and process important real-time data, like user interactions or session completions. By processing this data as quickly as it comes in, the company is able to spot anomalies and detect unusual user behavior, identifying critical issues within minutes. Amazon Kinesis Data Firehose, on the other hand, is used to easily collect data from other sources, such as third-party APIs, and load it into Amazon Redshift in near real time.

According to Burket, building its platform on AWS’s scalable analytics infrastructure prepared Duolingo for the unforeseeable and unprecedented spike in learners during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Increased Demand during a Pandemic Onset

“Duolingo’s traffic has increased every year since we launched, and we typically see annual growth spikes around New Year’s Day and the back-to-school season. So we’ve gotten a lot of practice for handling growth in our infrastructure,” says Burket. “But even that paled in comparison to the kind of sudden growth we were talking about earlier this year.”

Learners flocked to Duolingo to supplement their remote education or find a new quarantine hobby, and the platform needed to scale to handle that influx of traffic and provide a high-quality language education. The Duolingo team members had to think on their feet and strategize how to meet this unprecedented need. “The new traffic meant we had more data. To be able to handle that volume, we really just needed more storage,” Burket explains. “Amazon Redshift had just recently released its RA3 instances with managed storage, offering double the storage capacity with roughly the same size Redshift cluster.”

“We have been able to grow a lot of our infrastructure remarkably cost efficiently over the years. And I think a lot of that has been to do with the fact that the Amazon Redshift team keeps shipping performance improvements nonstop,” Burket says. “The service has grown in a way that has continued to meet our performance needs without us needing to spend tons of money on it, which has been really delightful for us.”

Looking toward the Future of Language Learning

As Duolingo grows, its team has plans to expand how it uses data to improve the learning experience. Building on AWS, according to Burket, enables Duolingo to centralize and test its ideas in one location while scaling based on demand.

“On a daily basis, we can analyze 300 different things we’re trying at once, aggregating the data and producing summary reports on our tests—how things are doing, where things are going really well, and where things aren’t going so well,” Burket says. “Just the magnitude of our capacity to run these analyses has been central to how we grow. And a lot of that has been enabled by the new types of things we were able to do over our data.”

In the future, Duolingo hopes to offer more-personalized content on its platform, as well as perform tests to improve performance and usability. As the demand for virtual education increases, the company hopes to incorporate more machine learning into its analytics processes, enabling smarter insights and improving outcomes. And Burket is confident that this new infrastructure can help Duolingo accomplish its goals.
