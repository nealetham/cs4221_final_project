5 Reasons Cheaper By The Dozen (2003) Is The Best (& 5 Reasons The Sequel Is Better)

Cheaper by the Dozen is one of the greatest kids and family films of the 2000s. The original Cheaper by the Dozenwas a 1950 movie about Frank and Lillian Gilbreth and their 12 children and it was based on a true story. In 2003, the remake starred Steve Martin and Bonnie Hunt as Tom and Kate Baker.

The remake drew in a young audience, with popular talent like Hilary Duff, Tom Welling, and Alyson Stoner. Two years later, in 2005, a sequel was released in which the Baker family goes on a lakeside vacation and competes against Tom's arch-nemesis, Jimmy Murtaugh (Eugene Levy). These films are such a fixture of the early aughts that cast members recently relived some of their classic character moments for charity. Thankfully, the first movie is on Disney+. While the second is not currently there, it can still be rented or purchased on platforms such as Amazon Prime. Let's see where the original remake is best and where Cheaper by the Dozen 2excels.

2003: Setting Up The Family

✕ Remove Ads

Much is done in the first movie to set up the family structure. One of the best scenes in the film is Bonnie Hunt's monologue over a timeline video montage of meeting Tom, having their first three kids while working, and living life as the other nine children quickly came about.

The kids certainly act out when they are forced to move from Midland, Indiana, to Evanston, Illinois. Though they aren't perfect, they are presented creatively in the first movie.

2005: Summertime

Cheaper by the Dozen 2is the perfect summer family flick. Take a vacation at the lake vicariously through the Bakers, and you won't be disappointed.

The first movie necessarily focused on the structure, or lack thereof, of life during the academic year. It's a good contrast to see what the huge family is like in summer mode.

✕ Remove Ads

2003: Work-Life Balance

The first movie is built on the difficulties of maintaining a work-life balance, which Tom and Kate are pretty bad at. It is important for Kate to pursue her career dreams as a published author, but it's also unrealistic for Tom to be a full-time college football coach and successfully take care of so many kids alone when Kate is gone.

However realistic or unrealistic you find the whole situation, it's at least interesting to explore.

2005: Competition

✕ Remove Ads

Cheaper by the Dozenis not totally without competition. Tom has something to prove to "Shake," his college buddy who climbed to the top in college athletics and hired him. Tom and Kate even get a little competitive about whose career should thrive.

RELATED: Steve Martin's 10 Best Movies According to IMDb

However, the second movie is the place to go for good old field day competition. Tom turns everything into a contest between his family and the Murtaughs, so the suspense is real.

2003: Teenage Struggles

By the second movie, Tom Welling and Hilary Duff are even more grown up. While Welling's character, Charlie, does explore a love interest, Duff's Lorraine spends most of the movie sitting in a lounge chair and reading magazines.

They showed a lot more angst in the first film as the teens struggled to fit in at a new high school, especially Charlie on the football team.

✕ Remove Ads

2005: Steve & Eugene

It doesn't get much better than Steve Martin and Eugene Levy. These comedy greats are perfect together. Even though Tom Baker derided his kids' meat-soaking of Hank/Ashton Kutcher's undies in the first movie, the dad teams up with Sarah (Alyson Stoner) to reprise the prank at the Murtaughs' home.

RELATED: Eugene Levy: 10 Best Roles, According To IMDb

Levy and Martin's rivalry is silly but golden. Another great example is the campfire sing-off they pull their families into on the lake.

2003: Belonging

✕ Remove Ads

Watching Cheaper by the Dozenas an adult makes all the funny scenes funnier, but it also makes the sad scenes sadder. Mark Baker is repeatedly called "Fed-ex" because his siblings don't think he fits in with the family. They joke that the Fed-ex guy dropped him off. This is dark stuff for a kids' movie, and it's horrific that one's brother and sister could ever say such things.

The first film really hones in on Mark's loneliness and how he is affected by the other kids' meanness (spoiler alert: he runs away from home). The second movie doesn't get this deep with any of the younger kids.

2005: Blossoming

Even though she is terrible to Mark in the first movie, Sarah Baker gets more of the spotlight in the second film. It's still not as deep as being totally left out of everything, but Sarah's growing pains are worthy of screen time.

✕ Remove Ads

Alyson Stoner's character falls for Taylor Lautner's Elliot Murtaugh, and the two go on a date to the movies. After Sarah shoplifts some makeup from a store at the lake to prepare for her date, she gets some help from her mom and big sister Lorraine, who understand what she is going through. It was good for young tween girls to see Sarah continue to be her tomboy self and pursue a relationship she was interested in.

2003: Dylan Is Beans

Even though Mark's pet frog in the first movie is Beans, the original Beans himself is also present. Steven Anthony Lawrence, who played Beans on Even Stevens, plays the role of Dylan Shenk. Dylan's dad is pretty cool, but his mom is a little overprotective and rude. Dylan just wants to be like other kids and have fun, but he is injured every time that happens, unfortunately.

✕ Remove Ads

The crazy chandelier scene when the Bakers first move in is one of the best parts of the movie. In an interview with the author, Lawrence said that this role was his first time roller-blading!

2005: A Baby Is Born

The first modern Cheaper by the Dozeninstallment ends with the family enjoying Christmas dinner right as the chandelier crashes to the ground again. The second one has an arguably better ending. Fans are pleased to see that eldest daughter Nora (Piper Perabo) has left Hankfor good.

She and her husband, Bud (Jonathan Bennett), are expecting their first child. Pregnant Nora is good comedic relief for much of the film, but she really brings everyone together when the baby is born. It's pretty sweet that she and Bud name the baby boy after her dad, Tom.
