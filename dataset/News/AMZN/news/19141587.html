    OTT players oppose DCCC regulation model

    OTT players oppose DCCC regulation model

    Synopsis

    OTT players including Netflix, Amazon, MXPlayer, Zee, AltBalaji and Arre believe it is adequate to have companies redressing complaints on their own and there is no need to have DCCC.

    BENGALURU: Most video streaming platforms have opposed the idea of an institutional self-regulatory model similar to traditional media, citing a host of laws that already exist in the country to regulate problematic content. It includes content such as obscenity, indecent representation of women or anything that may be perceived as anti-national.

    Recently, Hotstar, Jio, SonyLiv and Eros formed an adjudicatory body Digital Content Complaint Council (DCCC), which will be headed by a retired judge and have representations from industry and the government. Amazon Prime has refused to join the new council. Over-the-top (OTT) players including Netflix, Amazon, MXPlayer, Zee, AltBalaji and Arre believe it is adequate to have companies redressing complaints on their own and there is no need to have DCCC, which is similar to Broadcasting Content Complaints Council (BCCC). They feel that self regulatory model followed by TV can’t be adopted by OTT players as TV is for family viewing purposes while OTT is an individual viewing medium.

    Information and Broadcasting Minister Prakash Javadekar on Monday gave the industry a hundred days to set up an adjudicatory body and finalise a code of conduct. “Everyone including the ministry has agreed that any mechanism finally should be formulated in consultation with the whole industry including the creators. The whole industry should be aligned on it,” said Karan Bedi, CEO at MX Player. MX Player is owned by Times Internet, a part of the Times Group which also publishes this newspaper.

    “We believe that for such mechanism to be truly effective for the consumer, substantial number of OTT services, both at a regional and national level, should be signatories to this process and implement the same,” said Tarun Katial, CEO, ZEE5 India.

    Players opposing the government’s push believe that there must be as little regulation of content as possible because OTT industry already follows the Indian Penal Code, Criminal Procedure Code, Information Technology Act and Cable Act as well as a host of other laws. Non-signatories believe that DCCC would provide a comparative advantage to platforms that mostly rely on TV content that is already compliant with BCCC rules. Sony told ET last week that it endorses the Ministry’s recommendation that the best way forward is to let the internet and overthe-top industry self-regulate by emulating the “successful” model of self-regulation put in place by the BCCC.
