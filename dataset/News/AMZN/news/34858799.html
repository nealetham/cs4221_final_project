The best board games for kids offer the perfect solution to the age-old “I’m bored” refrain and provide excellent opportunities for family bonding—not to mention family rivalries. Board games draw kids away from popular screen-based activities, such as video games, and teach life skills, including cooperation, patience and winning and losing gracefully, as well as counting, spatial reasoning and problem solving. Another bonus: They won’t take up too much room in your toy storage areas.

You don’t have to overthink your board game shopping, as Dr. Rebekah Diamond, author of Parent Like a Pediatrician explains. “It’s really how you engage with a game that’s most important, and there’s no need to worry about finding a game specifically designed to promote development or learning,” she says. “When I look for games, my biggest goal is to find something I will actually want to play myself.” In addition, think about whether you want a competitive or cooperative game. (It’s nice to have both options in your board game arsenal.)

We’ve rounded up the best board games for kids to entertain all ages, from toddlers to teenagers, plus a couple of picks for family game nights. We’ve put a premium on fun in selecting these games for kids. Best Board Games For Toddlers

A Simple Memory Game That Celebrates World Cultures

MOST POPULAR

I Never Forget a Face

Designed for kids age 3 and up, I Never Forget a Face contains 24 matching pairs of friendly faces from 24 countries ranging from Vietnam to India to Norway. At 2.25 inches square, the cards are just the right size for little ones to hold—not too big and not too small.

If you have a younger toddler interested in the game, you can easily make it more manageable by removing some of the pairs; then, add them as they develop their memory and concentration skills. Up to four players can take part in the fun, and because matching games can even challenge grown-ups’ brains, they’ll hold a parent’s interest, too. Bonus: The cards are made from 90% recycled materials and printed with vegetable-based inks.

An Easy Cooperative Game For Toddlers To Enjoy

Snail's Pace Race

A round of the award-winning Snail’s Pace Race game takes 15 minutes—ideal for preschoolers’ short attention spans. It only requires two players but accommodates up to six, so the whole family can get together for some (slow) racing fun.

The word “race” doesn’t quite fit this game, as it’s a cooperative effort; even if you have fewer than six players, you’ll use all six snails. Snail’s Pace Race is simple; players roll the dice to move the snails along until they reach the end. While your child is enjoying the game, they’ll also practice counting, color-matching and more.

Best Board Games For Kids In Preschool

This One-Player Puzzle Game Offers Multiple Levels Of Play

Smart Farmer

The one-player board game Smart Farmer offers an opportunity for kids age 4 and up to spend some quiet time alone while developing their problem-solving skills. Still, it can work even better with a parent to lend a hand. The game includes a book with 60 logic-based challenges that instruct players to separate the multiple cows, horses, pigs and sheep into different areas of the board using fences (somewhat like a 3-D version of the classic fox-chicken-grain puzzle). The challenges are categorized by skill level: Starter, Junior, Expert and Master, and the final scenarios can be tough even for older kids and adults.

Smart Farmer’s board and pieces are sufficiently well-made for kids’ imaginative farmyard play, too.

A Bingo-Based Game With A Visual Twist

Zingo!

Zingo!, which expands on the classic game of Bingo, is a competitive matching game designed to help pre-readers and early readers develop language and memory skills. Each of the 72 Zingo! cards contain two difficulty levels, with one on each side. When the Zinger dispenses tiles with pictures and short words (cake, ball, apple and so on), players match them to squares on their bingo, er, Zingo! cards. The first person to cover their game board yells “Zingo!” and is the winner.

This game takes less than 30 minutes to play (depending on your child’s skill level, of course) and accommodates two or more players age 4 and up. It tends to be a big hit at the preschool and kindergarten ages.

A Colorful Barnyard Game For Preschoolers

Count Your Chickens!

Another cooperative game, Count Your Chickens teaches young kids the basics of board games while they work together to hustle all those chicks back to the coop. Two to four players can join the fun in this game designed for ages 3 and up. Our parenting editor found it to be a big hit around age 4.

Best Board Games For Kids Ages 5–7

A Longtime Favorite For Younger School-Age Kids

Hasbro Guess Who?

Millennials and younger Gen-Xers might have fond childhood memories of playing Guess Who? with friends and family members, making it the sort of game they won’t mind playing with their kids. Since very few of us are likely to have held on to the game from 20 to 30 years ago, here’s your chance to get your family their own (complete with updated characters). For those whose childhoods were Guess Who?-less, one player picks a character, and the other guesses which one by asking questions about him or her, such as “Does your person wear glasses?”

The game includes 24 mystery cards and 48 face cards and has a more diverse group of characters than the original version of the game featured.

This Fast-Paced Matching Game

Spot-It!

Spot-It is a crowd-pleaser, starting in preschool, but it really takes off as they get a touch older (and faster). It also makes a smart birthday gift for younger elementary school-age children. While it’s very straightforward and easy to play, the game is appropriate for a broad age range.

Each Spot It game is actually five games in one; depending on which version you choose to play, the object is to either get rid of all your cards or collect the most. We typically play The Tower version. It feels the simplest, and everyone loves it. All the players start with a single card while the deck sits face-up in the middle. Each card carries multiple symbols and pictures, and players try to spot matching symbols on the upturned deck. Whoever calls it out first, snags the winning card. Each round takes about 15 minutes, and up to eight family members can play. Besides this “classic” version, the company offers other versions of Spot It that are sure to meet at least one of your kid’s interests, including Paw Patrol, Frozen IIand Marvel.

Best Board Games For Kids Ages 8–12

A Silly Game To Make The Whole Family LOL

Kids Against Maturity

You’ll notice that the name of the award-winning game Kids Against Maturity is inspired by the extremely non-kid-friendly Cards Against Humanity (which I recommend for post-bedtime laughs), but don’t worry, this deck doesn’t include any cards that read “[redacted]” or “[redacted].” (That said, Kids Against Maturity’s website jokes that “Humorless hover-parents and those who don’t appreciate flatulence jokes may not approve.”) And yes, there are quite a few poop and fart jokes involved in this one, but rarely have I see a group of kids laugh so hard as when they’re playing Kids Against Maturity.

This simple game requires a player to draw a Question card and the remaining players to each choose one of their randomly dealt Answer cards. The Question asker chooses the funniest Answer card, which is given to the player who chose it, and at the end of the game, the person with the most is the winner. Kids Against Maturity is recommended for players age “10 to 110.”

An Oldie-But-A-Goodie

Mancala

Mancala is one of the oldest two-player games in the world, and it’s not surprising that it’s still popular, because it’s easy to play and easy to get competitive over. Plus, with all the plastic toys and games kids have, it’s nice to use something made from natural materials once in a while. Mancala doesn’t take long to learn, and game play is simple, although your success depends on strategy rather than luck. Players move single pieces or groups of their 24 designated stones around the board, and the first to empty their side of the board is the winner.

This affordable game is recommended for kids age 6 and up, but when I play it with my 12-year-old son, it’s just as fun for him as for me (and I’ll admit that he often beats me).

This Classic Introduction To Strategy

Checkers Board for Kids

Checkers is a classic board game for kids for a reason—it’s dead simple and yet, offers them a glimpse into strategic game play. The recommended age is 8 and up, but our parenting editor has a 7-year-old who can’t get enough of this two-player classic. You may be able to introduce it as young as 6 to some. This set comes with the iconic black and red game pieces and a foldable board. It’s all a bit more lightweight than an adult set but feels just right for kids.

Best Board Games For Teens

A Party Game For Screen-Free Fun With Friends

Codenames

Codenames can be played with two players, but “competitive” play is best with at least two teams of two people each. If your teen has a big group of friends, no problem: Codenames has no limit on the number of people per team. The game is appropriate for adults, too—it’s one of the favorite games of one of my mom friends. (That’s probably why Codenames is in the top 15 products at Amazon in the board games category.)

In game play, which uses word associations and combinations, two spymasters, one on each team, know 25 agents’ secret identities, while their team members solely know the agents’ codenames. The object is to be the first team that contacts all of their agents without meeting the assassin. If you do, your team is out of the game. Each round takes about 15 minutes. (An aside for Disney fans: The company also offers a Disney edition.)

A Quirky Card Game For Teens

Unstable Unicorns

Unstable Unicorns is similar to the very popular card game Exploding Kittens (and both of them got their start on Kickstarter), but takes longer to play, accommodates more players (2 to 8) and has a higher age recommendation of 14+, which makes it a great pick for teens. The game’s tagline is “Build a Unicorn Army. Betray Your Friends. Unicorns Are Your Friends Now.”

In Unstable Unicorns, your goal is to collect unicorns to build an army while destroying other players' unicorns with certain cards. Depending on the number of players, the winner is the first person to build a stable of six or seven unicorns.

Best Board Games For Families To Play Together

A Cooperative Game For Young Detectives

Outfoxed!

When my 12-year-old was younger, he played this game at his grandparents’ house and really enjoyed it. Cooperative games are always nice for a change of pace, especially when you’re playing with little kids still working on not being sore losers. In this one, a naughty fox has stolen a pot pie, and players work together to find him, meaning that Outfoxed! is a fun way to inspire some family teamwork.

In each turn, you either collect evidence or a suspect and decide where you’ll move your character. You can eliminate suspects with the “clue decoder.” This game is designed for children age 5 and up and for two or more players. It takes about 20 minutes, but it isn’t the most straightforward game at the outset. Given everyone a bit of time to nail the concept. (Another cooperative game I recommend is Race to the Treasure, which is simpler to play.)

A Popular Family Game For Train Lovers—Or Any Board Game Fan

Ticket To Ride

In the award-winning game Ticket to Ride, players earn points by claiming railway routes between two cities, completing a continuous path of routes from their destination tickets and claiming the longest continuous path of routes. The winner is the person who has earned the most points by the end of the game.

This version of Ticket to Ride (several more are available) is set in North America and highlights major cities like Las Vegas, New York and Montreal. It includes 225 colored pieces to build trains, along with 144 illustrated cards. The recommended minimum age is 8 years, and it takes 2 to 5 players about 30 to 60 minutes to play. However, it can be a lengthy game at times, depending on the age of your kids. Our parenting editor’s children often leave it set up overnight, returning to play over several days, depending on their interest.

Charades With Picture-Based Cards So All Ages Can Play

Charades For Kids

Charades tends to be popular with all ages and this picture-based version lets little ones in on the fun. Square off into teams this holiday season with Charades for Kids, and I imagine everyone will be giggling by the end. Each card contains a picture of at least one of possible actions, or charades, to be performed, so kids as young as 4 may be able to “read” it on their own and act it out, rather than asking a parent for help.

What Is The Most Popular Kid Board Game?

Many of today’s most popular kid-friendly board games are longtime family favorites, such as Candy Land, Checkers, Monopoly and Sorry!, while others are newer to the scene (and included in our picks above), such as Zingo! and Codenames.

Which Game Is Best For Children?

When looking at potential games, the age recommendation can help ensure safety and appropriate language and topics. “Choking hazards are something to be aware of at different ages and should be something you can find on the boxes,” says Diamond. “If you stick to something close to the right age recommendation for your kid, there shouldn’t be any issues with a game that is inappropriate with content.”

Are Board Games Good For Kids?

“Games and toys that are interactive and meant to be shared with the whole family are inherently wonderful for development,” says Diamond. That development can include improving problem solving or math skills, for example—plus, it takes kids away from their screens.

What Is The Best Game To Play Indoors?

Games that work best indoors are typically those with small or irreplaceable pieces that are easy to lose, while naturally, the best backyard toys and games are those that are too big, too messy or too rowdy for inside play.
