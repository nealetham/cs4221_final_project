*Cloud Natives Aren’t All The Same

If you talked about ‘the cloud’ two decades ago, people would look up at the sky. Today, most people in the digitally connected world understand that ‘the cloud’ refers to some faraway server farm where data is stored. But now, there’s another term to contend with: cloud native.

That’s not a guy with a loincloth and spear floating in some fuzzy firmament, but rather computer infrastructure and applications that are built primarily in the cloud, remotely, by writing code on virtual servers over the Internet.

The ability to build applications – pieces of software that do everything from track your calorie intake to transferring money – on a laptop without ever installing a server room or renting a rack at a data center has revolutionized application development and led to a dizzying array of new products and services.

But not everyone wants to go native in the same way. Many developers insist on building their applications on virtual machines in the cloud that they control while CTOs and CEOs are more inclined to take advantage of the multitude of cloud-based, back-end services now delivered by application programming interfaces, or APIs.

"Building cloud native applications means creating software that is designed with the advantages—and disadvantages—of the cloud in mind," said Klint Finley, former Wired reporter and the author of a recent report by the application platform company Lightbend - Cloud Native Adoption Trends 2020-2021.

It turns out that most developers still prefer systems they can personally maintain and scale, while the business side clearly prefers systems that are delivered “as a service” via API, the survey says. Consuming back-end services by API rather than building and maintaining your own is the defining characteristic of the emergent cloud.

"It means taking advantage of the fact that it’s possible to outsource entire categories of functionality—like databases and authentication—to public cloud services and planning for the fact that communication between those cloud components might be unreliable," Finley said.

Twenty years ago, big tech companies like IBM, Sun Microsystems, HP, Cisco and Intel began talking about distributed networks, calling them ‘utility computing’ or ‘grid computing.’ Then in 2007, Amazon launched Elastic Compute Cloud, the first large public cloud, allowing engineers to manage virtual computers through an Internet interface. On the back end, however, Amazon split up functionality and sent it to data centers spread all over the globe.

Initially, companies used the cloud for backup storage, but quickly began migrating primary infrastructure to the virtual machines. Today Amazon’s cloud, Amazon Web Services, is a $40 billion annual business and market research firm Gartner estimates that the global cloud market is worth as much as $263 billion today.

Companies now specialize in building microservices that software developers can access through the cloud and assemble into applications. Ligthbend co-founder and CTO Jonas Bonér predicts that developers will eventually become more like general contractors, “pulling together the pieces that have been built by subcontractors into a cohesive product.”

Consider Newegg, an electronics e-commerce company whose website is among the top 200 web sites in the U.S. ranked by internet traffic and engagement, according to Amazon’s web traffic analysis subsidiary Alexa.

Montaque Hou, Newegg’s CTO, says that while his company builds applications in their own data center, they integrate cloud-based services such as Google Translate and Google Cloud Vision into their apps using APIs.

“The cloud is way more expensive than our on-premise data center,” said Mr. Hou, adding that his site handles 100 million individual products. “But we do like some cloud offerings, which are unique, as a supplement to our business.”

Consuming back-end services by API rather than building and maintaining them in house, whether with on-premise servers or in the cloud, is a very different approach than the old-school, server-side mindset that still controls vast legacy systems at most major enterprises.

The Lightbend survey of more than 1,000 developers, software architects, and IT leaders found that three quarters of respondents already host the majority of their applications in some sort of cloud infrastructure. The survey suggests that while management prefers outsourcing as much as possible, developers prefer not losing too much control.

One advantage of building an application in the cloud is that the infrastructure and the application can scale with demand, expanding and contracting without interrupting responsiveness to the user. The cloud may be more expensive, but handling high capacity at a datacenter, on premises or not, requires building for peak traffic and letting machines sit idle when traffic drops.

The flexibility of the cloud also allows developers to roll out updates in a much more seamless fashion as opposed to bringing down the whole system to do a rollout as is necessary in a non-cloud native world. That’s important these days when speed is a critical competitive advantage. Streaming service Netflix deploys new code as much as 4,000 times a day.

“Take advantage of what's out there,” said Lightbend’s Bonér. “If there's a user authentication and identification service that meets your requirements, by all means you should be using it.”
