Beyond Zoom U: The Online Learning Experience Students Want

Scott Pulsipher

The adoption of online education had been slow and steady—until the pandemic hit this spring. Millions of students emptied out of physical campuses and thousands of schools worked night and day to enable online course delivery within days or weeks. But now institutions face an even tougher challenge: to deliver a product that meets student expectations and takes full advantage of what technology has to offer. 

For many students, the Covid-19-driven online learning experiment has so far fallen flat, with seven out of ten rating their online courses as worse than the in-person classes in which they were initially enrolled. Students pursue college for a range of reasons, an issue thoughtfully explored in Choosing College by Michael Horn and Bob Moesta.Those reasons include learning, academics, and career investment, but also the pursuit of the campus-based experience for its own sake. Online education—short on student unions, intramural sports, or even rock-climbing walls—is an incredible medium for teaching and learning, but it can be far from student expectations associated with on-campus life.  

But, as with other online experiences, when it comes to learning, not everything online is created equal. The mad-but-admirable scramble to move class discussions to Zoom or post lectures on YouTube was the fast and easy solution in a moment of crisis, but it underutilizes the rich, immersive benefits that only technology can offer. Schools that hope to continue to charge top-dollar tuition as their model moves online need to consider what differentiates top-flight online programs.

Designing for Student Support 

Taking a digitally native approach to education centers everything around the benefit to the student. It is about teaching each individual, not about broadcasting content to the masses. High-quality online provides one-to-one engagement, effective virtual group learning, coaching and advising, and the right content at the right time—rather than lectures on set schedules.

In the unsettled times in which we live, relationships matter more than ever. Online teaching opens possibilities beyond the ‘sage on the stage’ model, creating space for instructional teams that can address students’ academic needs and better meet their need for support, guidance, and mentorship. This frees up faculty to help students individually, when and where they need it, rather than leading a class march at a one-size-fits-none pace.

 Give Students Ownership of Time 

Students finishing high school today cannot remember a time when shopping was limited by store hours, when hailing a taxi meant spotting one in the street, or when they couldn’t get food delivered at the click of a button. Amazon, Uber, and DoorDash have transformed our consumer experiences to focus on speed and convenience—and students bring expectations from those experiences to online education.

Online learning can give students control of their schedules: students can access course materials anytime, anywhere, on-demand. As 24/7 increasingly becomes the norm, we must contemplate a model that moves beyond a paradigm of office hours on faculty time and enables not just asynchronous access to content, but on-demand access to instruction and tutoring. Ultimately, online learning shifts ownership of time from institutions to students.

Giving students control of their time doesn’t just provide speed and convenience—it shifts the focus from completing credit hours to learning and mastery. Learners absorb different concepts at varying rates, with some able to move on to new material more quickly and others needing more time to achieve mastery. This has implications for the way we structure and fund higher education: there is no need for 15-week terms and organizing financial aid through credit hours in a world of self-paced, 24/7 learning.

Data-Driven Personalization and Continuous Improvement 

Almost by definition, technology products improve faster than their analog equivalents. As consumers, we expect that the iPhone 10 is faster, sleeker, and more functional than the first iteration of the smartphone. Well-designed online learning models incorporate this need to improve into their architecture. The best models are built to take in a wealth of data on student learning to drive continuous improvement of the learning experience. More specifically, they enable randomized controlled trials to assess efficacy of content, learning resources, course sequencing, faculty interactions, peer engagements, and more.

Rich data analytics like this are seldom available in classroom-based environments—and unfortunately, aren’t available to many online programs where lecture-delivery is the primary design. Using the online environment to its full potential means purposefully gathering and analyzing data to personalize the learning journey and analyze impact of activities and interactions. It increases faculty and institutional accountability for student success and begins to recast the measures of quality towards student value and success.

 Summer Assignments 

Institutions who are new to online have an important decision: given the risks that could prevent students from being physically present in the fall, should they invest in a rich online learning environment, or continue with what they were able to quickly build when Covid-19 hit in March? Truly taking advantage of the technology available to us creates tremendous benefits for students: it enables student-centered design, it gives students ownership of their time, and it gives institutions the data to become expert at teaching and learning. Increasingly, schools will be judged not by their campuses, selectivity and reputations, but rather by whether they provide robust, technology-enabled, personalized learning experiences and continually increase student access and outcomes.
