10 Plot Holes In Amazon's Tales From The Loop

Amazon Prime's Tales From The Loop is the most recent show that sci-fi fans are flocking to. Often incorrectly likened to Netflix'sStranger Things, this new series from Amazon is unique in its understated, mellow aesthetic, quiet and thoughtful dialogue and melancholic tone.

It's certainly not the lightest experience, but there is much beauty to be found in Tales From The Loop. The show prides itself on using elements of the fantastic as vessels to explore human subjects, and it does it well. That being said, some of the fantastical elements are left a little too vague, with the ties in storylines hanging on by a thread.

Loretta Tells Herself Too Much

If adult Loretta had kept things vague when she met young Loretta, it would make much more sense that their time loop would continue to happen. Young Loretta meets older Loretta and then goes on with her life, convincing herself it's a dream, just to become an adult and meet her young self. But older Loretta is so thorough in telling her young self that she will be convinced it's a dream, but it really isn't, that it's unlikely young Loretta would make that mistake again, therefore changing the series of events.

Loretta Would Recognize Her House And Son

Young Loretta unknowingly walks into the house of her older self while spending a day with a boy who is actually her future son. She later finds out this fact. Yet somehow when she gets older, she fashions her house to look the same way and doesn't realize that the child she has is the boy she spent that day with. Loretta isn't an average person, she's a higher-up at The Loop, meaning she should be in tune to spectacular phenomena.

Jakob Could Have Told His Grandfather

When Jakob's friend Danny refuses to switch back into each other's rightful bodies, Jakob panics. He wants to go back to how life was before and he feels stuck. But he knows that the machine which switched their bodies likely came from The Loop, and his Grandfather runs The Loop.

The show chooses to ignore this for the sake of taking another, more tragic path with the narrative. The fact remains that it was a gapingly obvious way out of a predicament which would have granted the show some much-needed relief of tension.

Loretta's Time Travel

When Loretta's mother goes missing, she goes looking for her at The Loop, where she works. The people there say nobody works there by her mother's name, and viewers eventually find out that Loretta seems to have traveled forwards in time...but it's never explained how, since as the start of the episode she was having a normal day at school.

How Did Loretta Get Back To Her Own Time?

When she asks older Loretta what she should do now, her older self tells her, with a wise, kind smile, to go home - as if this is supposed to be touching, or obvious, or symbolic of something. In actuality, it's mostly just vague and covers up the lack of explanation for how young orphaned Loretta will take care of herself. She essentially sends herself to go stand in a snowy field and hope for the best.

The People In Stasis

May discovers agadget that allows her to pause time indefinitely. She never considers what effects this may have on people, and the show doesn't either.

The people are made to pause in place for months, and even if their human needs, such as eating and exercising and using the toilet, are paused, the world around them is not. After such a time, houses would be dirtied and the seasons would have changed, but this isn't considered.

No One Notices Jakob

It takes a very, very long time for anybody to notice that something is up with Jakob, who is actually Danny. For people who live in a town riddled with reality-defying objects and robots roaming the woods, one would think they would be more alert. Cole finally picks up on it, which is odd considering they live in a house where both of the parents work at The Loop and should know better.

✕ Remove Ads

The Echo Sphere

Audiences are introduced to the Echo Sphere when Cole's grandfather shows it to him. This is an explicit example of the fact that the grandfather is aware of the presence of random gadgets all over their town, and that it is considered so normal that he casually shows a young child. If this is the case, it makes little sense that nobody is shown talking about issues of safety and risks which clearly affect the town children.

Danny Doesn't Go For Help

✕ Remove Ads

Danny sees that the pod which switched him and Jakob is being destroyed, and at this point, he apparently feels sorry for refusing to switch back as Jakob - in Danny's body - is in the hospital. Danny knows very well that Jakob's grandfather would likely have a way to help if only he asked him, but yet again, this blaring fact is overlooked as if the characters had no choices.

No Status Quo Between Supernatural And Humans

One of the main things that the creator of The Loop, Nathaniel Halpern, stresses is his admiration of the paintings that were the influence for the show. He was drawn to them for the combination of sci-fi and human life in a way that made the sci-fi aspects unremarkable as if the humans were used to it. Yet in his show, the humans are only used to it until they need help; at which point they behave as if they are in any other typical sci-fi show where no one would believe them. The inconsistency is confusing and at times feels like a narrative cop-out.
