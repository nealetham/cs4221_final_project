Jeff Bezos says Amazon profit will be entirely wiped out by spending on coronavirus safety

The pugnacious founder warned investors 'take a seat' as he pledged to plough $4bn into cleaning testing, and protective equipment

Io Dodds Business Reporter, San Francisco
30 April 2020 9:40pm BST

Amazon disappointed investors on Thursday night as it said the coronavirus pandemic would wipe out its entire profits for the next three months despite skyrocketing sales.

Jeff Bezos, the online shopping giant's chief executive, warned shareholders to "take a seat" as he pledged a massive new health and safety push that sent shares tumbling by as much as 5pc in after-hours trading.

The company expects to make about $4bn (£3.2bn) in the current financial quarter, all of which will now be ploughed into higher pay, protective equipment, deep cleaning and mass coronavirus testing – its first operating loss since the summer of 2014.

It comes as Amazon struggles with a tidal wave of demand for online delivery, driving its revenue past Wall Street's expectations to $75.5bn while earnings per share were lower than hoped.

Mr Bezos said: "If you’re a shareowner in Amazon, you may want to take a seat, because we’re not thinking small."Under normal circumstances, in this coming Q2, we’d expect to make some $4 billion or more in operating profit. But these aren’t normal circumstances.

How Big Tech has coped with coronavirus

"Instead, we expect to spend the entirety of that $4 billion, and perhaps a bit more, on Covid-related expenses, getting products to customers and keeping employees safe."

"There is a lot of uncertainty in the world right now, and the best investment we can make is in the safety and well-being of our hundreds of thousands of employees.

"I’m confident that our long-term oriented shareowners will understand and embrace our approach, and that in fact they would expect no less.”

It was a characteristically unapologetic announcement for Mr Bezos, who has a long history of prioritising the growth of the company over short-term profits, betting that investors will stay on board.

The pugnacious founder, who is the world's richest man with an estimated net worth of $139.7bn, has taken personal charge of Amazon's anti-coronavirus effort after a period of delegation, perhaps aware that the company's response to this crisis may permanently alter his reputation.

Brian Olsavsky, Amazon's chief financial officer, added that it expects to spend about $1bn on testing this year, and that it has now hired finished hiring the 175,000 extra workers it said it would need this month.

He promised an end to "essential item" restrictions, which have maintained capacity but rendered the company's traditional promise of speedy delivery a distant dream, within the next three months.

The return of next-day delivery, however, may take longer. Data from SimilarWeb, an online analytics firm, indicated that Amazon's rate of converting web visits to actual sales was below that of US competitors, who appeared to be benefiting from its overload.

It is not clear whether the promise of higher wages refers to or adds to Amazon's previous commitment to increase warehouse workers' pay by $2, £2 or 2 euros per hour. The company refused to say how many of its facilities had suffered coronavirus cases.

Amazon's physical grocery stores also enjoyed a boost, with sales rising by 8pc after a long runof 1pc growth, mostly due to home delivery. Despite capacity increases of around 60pc, the company said there had been "more demand than capacity".

The crisis has also tested Amazon's record on worker safety and labour rights, with warehouse workers across the world filing petitions, walking out of work or simply failing to appear for work due to fear of infection.

Earlier this month, executives were accused of plotting a smear campaign" against a warehouse worker in New York City who had led a small staff rebellion, who has alleged that he was wrongly fired.

In Michigan, US, a 66-year-old woman in a nursing home fruitlessly begged for help from Amazon's voice assistant, Alexa, 44 times in the days leading up to her death from Covid-19.

Nicholas Hyett, an equity analyst at Hargreaves Lansdown, said that the move could be a "watershed" moment for Amazon's public image amid rising costs.

He said: "This is radical, and many management teams would baulk at the cost... [but] sacrificing a single quarter’s profits to secure political and public goodwill would have long term advantages.”
