As a senior in secondary school in Nigeria, I wanted to become a medical doctor — we all know how this turned out. To be fair, I got into pre-medical school, but realized in the second year that I was not designed to cut through the human body. After that, I changed schools to study computer science and we all know how that turned out. Still, I did not lose my attachment to improving medical science. My Bachelors thesis focused on using case-based reasoning³ for diagnosing typhoid fever in 2013. Which if I were to do all over, I will have leverage clinical notes with advances in Natural Language Understanding to predict typhoid fever cases in real time. Be on the lookout.

In the last couple of weeks, I have been searching for new use cases for Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels⁵, the amazing AI service from AWS — the greatest thing after sliced bread. After poring over Kaggle, I came across the chest x-ray images (pneumonia) dataset¹ and this was my Eureka moment. What if medical practitioners could leverage AI to diagnose pneumonia without having the compute power and technical expertise of Google⁴?

Hypothesis: What if medical practitioners could leverage AI to diagnose pneumonia without having the compute power and technical expertise of Google?

The Work

Preparing the dataset

To train a model with Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels⁵, I needed to have my dataset either on local and manually upload it via Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels console or already stored in an Amazon S3 bucket. Considering the size of the dataset and the tasks to be completed, I decided to leverage the power of the cloud — AWS. I launched my Amazon SageMaker Notebook, and installed Kaggle python library.

Next was to upload the images to an S3 location. However, I realized a glitch. Even though the information of the Kaggle dataset page showed 3 classes — Normal, Bacteria Pneumonia, and Viral Pneumonia, the folders were only split into 2 classes — Normal and Pneumonia. With a brief scan through the images, I realized they could be further split into 3 classes based on file names, thanks to the dataset¹ curators.

With the 3 classes in place, data was uploaded to my S3 bucket and ready to train on. This was the simplest data wrangling I have ever done for any computer vision task, less 11 lines of code.

After the above steps, I had to make the dataset available to Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels. I simply started by creating a new dataset in Amazon Custom Labels⁵ with a few clicks on the web console and pointing to my dataset location in S3. Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels gives you the opportunity to label your dataset right from the console. This I would only recommend if you have a handful of images. In my scenario, I had over 3k images and having split them into classes by folder structure, I had to check the “Automatically attach a label to my images based on the folder they’re stored in.” option so Amazon Rekognition could infer the labels automatically.

What I got:

With the dataset imported into Amazon Rekognition, it was time to train my medical diagnosis assistant.

Training the model

To train model in Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels, you start by creating a project.

Once the project was created, Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels was ready to train my model. In less than 5 clicks, I had my model training started. To do this, I:

  1. Selected the project,
  2. Chose my training dataset,
  3. Indicated that Amazon Rekognition should split the training dataset,
  4. Clicked the “Train” orange button, and
  5. Wait (most desirable attribute when training with machine learning models).

The Results

As I mentioned, I started to disprove my hypothesis by showing that it was not possible for medical practitioners to use AI to classify chest X-rays⁸ without being Google. I turned out to be wrong: It is indeed possible, thanks to Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels⁵. Let’s start by assessing how much I spent.

Costs

  1. The dataset was readily available online, which cost me next to nothing to download.
  2. My Amazon SageMaker Notebook instance cost me about $2, I used it for other tasks as well
  3. Amazon S3 costs is less than $.90
  4. Model training on Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels ran for 1.308 hours, at $1/hour; approximately $2.

Total training cost is about $5 ($4.90). This does not require Google financial power.

To make predictions with the trained model, it costs $4/hour on Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels. Hence, if I architect my production use case to make batch predictions 4 hours/day in a 28 day month, it would cost me about $450.00/month (scalability and infrastructure management inclusive). Thereby, helping thousands of medical practitioners around the world to improve pneumonia diagnosis and save millions of lives. See the greater good idea?

Model Metrics

I will not go into details to define each metric, I have already done this in a previous post⁷ and Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels⁵ documents these extensively. Technically, the model performance accuracy is 0.946 ~=94.6%.

In summary, the pneumonia diagnosis model trained on Amazon Rekognition Custom Labels on chest x-rays dataset¹ is able to correctly predict ~9 out 10 times if a patient has Bacterial Pneumonia, Viral Pneumonia or Zero Pneumonia case.

What’s more

Think of the magnitude and scale of benefit of this to health care across the world, even in the remotest parts. Think of medical use cases that can reuse this approach. Think of the lives we can save by augmenting X-ray diagnoses for medical practitioners with AI.
