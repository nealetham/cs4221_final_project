Apple app store: News publishers write to Tim Cook in wake of Fortnite spat

They are asking how members could qualify to get the same arrangement that Amazon has for its Prime Video app on the store

By Alys Key
August 21, 2020 4:10 pm(Updated August 24, 2020 7:21 pm)

News publishers have waded into the debate over the terms which Apple offers on its app store, joining a number of other companies in criticising the technology giant’s practices.

Digital Context Next (DCN), a group representing the likes of The Economist, The New York Times, and The Financial Times, has written to Apple’s chief executive Tim Cook asking how members could qualify to get the same arrangement that Amazon has for its Prime Video app on the store.

The deal in that case took a 15 per cent fee of customer subscriptions made via the app store, compared to the usual 30 per cent fee which Apple takes for in-app purchases.

DCN boss Jason Kint said Apple should share details of why Amazon was able to get this special rate, so that news publishers which meet the same conditions can be offered the same agreement.

“The terms of Apple’s unique marketplace greatly impact the ability to continue to invest in high-quality, trusted news and entertainment particularly in competition with other larger firms,” he said.

Battle for the app store

The letter cites Mr Cook’s response to US Representative Hank Johnson at an antitrust hearing which took place last month, in which Mr Cook, Facebook’s Mark Zuckerberg, Google’s Sundar Pichai and Amazon’s Jeff Bezos answered questions from lawmakers on the dominance of their respective platforms. Mr Cook said during the hearing that the deal given to Amazon was available to “anyone meeting the conditions”.

It comes at the same time as a major dispute over the app store’s terms has broken out, after the developers behind hit game Fortnite circumvented the in-app payment system to avoid what it saw as Apple’s unfair fees. The game was subsequently banned from the app store and its owner Epic Games hit back by launching an antitrust lawsuit seeking to establish the app store as a monopoly.

Music streaming service Spotify has backed Epic, issuing a statement saying: “Apple’s unfair practices have disadvantaged competitors and deprived consumers for far too long. The stakes for consumers and app developers large and small couldn’t be higher and ensuring that the iOS platform operates competitively and fairly is an urgent task with far-reaching implications.”

It is also a time of growing pressure for technology companies to pay news providers. Earlier this week, Google hit back at Australian proposals to make Google and Facebook pay news publishers.
